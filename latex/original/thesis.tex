\begin{abstract}
We study how reward shape and short-horizon temporal context interact in a DQN agent on a single multi-objective grid-survival task. The motivation is practical: multi-objective embodied control such as legged-robot locomotion and aerial navigation relies on dense per-step reward shaping with many weighted components, whose tuning is a known bottleneck. A $3 \times 2 \times 4$ ablation crosses three reward configurations (\textsc{baseline}, \textsc{absolute\_proximity}, \textsc{minimal}) with two observation types (single-frame and 4-frame stacked), four seeds per cell. The interaction is non-obvious. First, frame stacking has a reward-dependent direction: it adds $75$ ticks of mean survival to \textsc{minimal} but degrades \textsc{absolute\_proximity} and \textsc{baseline} by $47$ and $92$ ticks respectively. Second, without frame stacking, performance is largely independent of reward shape and clusters at $634$--$686$ mean survival. Third, the closest cell, \textsc{minimal} with frame stacking, reaches the heuristic within statistical noise (paired gap $-11$ ticks, not significantly different from zero). The reward-dependent direction is preliminary evidence that frame stacking isn't an unconditional remedy for under-performance: under the leakier rewards, the added observation history is used to collect more reward, but at the cost of survival. The findings are specific to this environment; we don't claim they generalize without replication elsewhere.\footnote{Source code and experiment configurations are available at \url{https://github.com/philippmerz/rl-thesis}.}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% =============================================================================

A reinforcement learning agent optimizes whatever reward function it is given. On tasks with several competing objectives (making progress while staying safe and managing limited resources), there is rarely a single scalar return that cleanly captures the intended behavior. The natural environment-driven reward (the robot fell, the episode timed out) is also sparse, arriving only at episode end and leaving many low-level actions to be credited from one terminal signal (the \emph{credit-assignment problem}). The standard response to both problems is to hand-design a dense per-step reward as a weighted sum of components and tune the weights until the learned policy matches the designer's intent. This tuning is a recognized practical bottleneck: published reward functions for multi-objective embodied control such as legged-robot locomotion and quadrotor flight combine multiple weighted components \citep{Hwangbo2019, Hwangbo2017}, and setting their relative magnitudes is widely reported as one of the main difficulties of training such policies.

Dense shaping introduces its own design problem. The relative magnitudes between components determine what the agent actually optimizes, and small changes can produce qualitatively different behaviors. Well-known failure modes called \emph{reward hacking} \citep{Amodei2016} emerge when the learned policy maximizes these dense rewards in unanticipated and unintended ways.

A natural complement to this reward design is the observation. Many environments expose only the current state, which carries position information but not motion. From a single frame, the agent can't tell an approaching threat from a retreating one. Frame stacking \citep{Mnih2015}, done by concatenating the last $k$ frames so the network can infer derived quantities like velocity directly, serves as the standard solution to this problem. However, the interaction between reward shape and frame stacking isn't well understood.

\paragraph{Scope.} This work studies how those two design axes (reward shape and short-horizon temporal context) interact in a DQN agent on a single multi-objective survival task. The task is deliberately small and controlled: a $64 \times 64$ gridworld with five discrete actions in which the agent must eat, evade enemies, and use shelters. Gridworlds isolate RL phenomena that are harder to disentangle in continuous-action embodied settings \citep{Leike2017, ChevalierBoisvert2023}. The findings reported here are properties of this environment and are not claimed to transfer mechanistically to the embodied control tasks that motivate the problem. The comparison target is a scripted heuristic baseline. We run a $3 \times 2 \times 4$ ablation crossing three reward configurations (\textsc{baseline}, \textsc{absolute\_proximity}, \textsc{minimal}, defined in Section~\ref{sec:reward_configs}) with two observation types (single-frame and 4-frame stacked), four random seeds per cell. The two \textsc{minimal} cells use a $50{,}000$-tick episode cap because the agent survives long enough that the $1{,}000$-tick cap used elsewhere would right-censor the cell mean (rationale in Section~\ref{sec:setup}). We add one illustrative single-seed run, \textsc{weak\_proximity}.

\paragraph{Findings.} \textsc{minimal} with frame stacking is the highest-performing of the six cells and reaches the heuristic within statistical noise: the paired gap is $-11$ ticks ($95\%$ CI $[-36, +15]$, $p = 0.41$), with mean survival $761$ ticks against the heuristic's $772$. The CI includes zero, so we cannot rule out parity, but we also cannot show that \textsc{minimal\_fs} exceeds the heuristic. The more informative result was one we had not set out to test: frame stacking does not improve every reward configuration uniformly but has a direction that depends on the reward. It adds $75$ ticks of mean survival to \textsc{minimal}, removes $47$ from \textsc{absolute\_proximity}, and removes $92$ from \textsc{baseline}. Adding observation history is therefore not an unconditional improvement under our experimental conditions. Under the two leakier reward designs studied here, it actively hurt. We discuss plausible mechanisms in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\paragraph{Agent and compute.} The agent is a Rainbow-lite DQN \citep{Hessel2018} combining Double DQN, a Dueling architecture, prioritized experience replay, and 3-step returns, along with stability techniques (LayerNorm, AdamW, Polyak target updates). Training runs on single consumer hardware, following work by \citet{ObandoCeron2021}, \citet{Schwarzer2023}, \citet{ObandoCeron2023}, and \citet{Clark2024} demonstrating that algorithmic choices can substitute for large-scale compute. The architecture, all hyperparameters except the frame-stack size, and the training budget are held constant across all twenty-four main-ablation runs.

Section~\ref{sec:background} reviews the relevant background. Section~\ref{sec:environment} describes the survival environment. Section~\ref{sec:architecture} details the agent architecture. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} presents the experiments, including failure-mode analysis, reward configurations, and the main ablation. Sections~\ref{sec:discussion} and~\ref{sec:conclusion} discuss findings and conclude.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning Fundamentals}

We formulate the task as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) defined by a tuple $(S, A, P, R, \gamma)$: state space $S$, action space $A$, transition function $P(s' \mid s, a)$, reward function $R(s, a, s')$, and discount factor $\gamma \in [0, 1)$ \citep{SuttonBarto2018}.

The agent interacts with the environment in discrete time steps $t = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$, receiving reward $r_t \in \mathbb{R}$ at each step. The goal is to find a policy $\pi: S \to A$ that maximizes the expected discounted return $G_t = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k r_{t+k+1}$. The action-value function
\begin{equation}
Q^\pi(s, a) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[G_t \mid s_t = s,\; a_t = a\right]
\end{equation}
gives the expected return of taking action $a$ in state $s$ and following $\pi$ thereafter. The optimal action-value function $Q^*(s, a) = \max_\pi Q^\pi(s, a)$ satisfies the Bellman optimality equation:
\begin{equation}
Q^*(s, a) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[r_t + \gamma \max_{a'\in A} Q^*(s_{t+1}, a')\right],
\end{equation}
where $s_{t+1}$ is the successor state reached after taking $a$ in $s$.

Q-learning estimates $Q^*$ directly through temporal difference (TD) updates:
\begin{equation}
Q(s_t, a_t) \leftarrow Q(s_t, a_t) + \alpha\!\left[r_t + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s_{t+1}, a') - Q(s_t, a_t)\right],
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is the learning rate. The bracketed quantity is the TD error $\delta_t$.

\subsection{Deep Q-Networks}

When the state space is large, tabular Q-learning is infeasible. Deep Q-Networks (DQN) approximate $Q^*$ with a neural network $Q(s, a; \theta)$ parameterized by weights $\theta$ \citep{Mnih2013, Mnih2015}.

Two mechanisms stabilize training. First, instead of sampling temporally correlated consecutive state transitions, which would violate the independence assumption that the optimizer relies on, \emph{experience replay} stores transitions $(s, a, r, s')$ in a buffer and samples mini-batches uniformly. Second, a \emph{target network} with parameters $\theta^-$ provides stable TD targets. The loss for a sampled transition is:
\begin{equation}
L(\theta) = \left[r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s', a'; \theta^-) - Q(s, a; \theta)\right]^2.
\end{equation}
The target parameters $\theta^-$ are periodically copied from $\theta$.

Exploration uses an $\varepsilon$-greedy policy: with probability $\varepsilon$ a random action is taken, otherwise the greedy action $\arg\max_a Q(s, a; \theta)$.

\subsection{DQN Extensions}
\label{sec:dqn_extensions}

Several extensions address specific weaknesses of the basic algorithm.

\paragraph{Double DQN.} Standard DQN overestimates action values because the same network both selects and evaluates the maximizing action. Double DQN \citep{vanHasselt2016} decouples these steps:
\begin{equation}
y = r + \gamma\, Q\!\left(s',\; \arg\max_{a'} Q(s', a'; \theta);\; \theta^-\right).
\end{equation}
The online network selects the action, the target network evaluates it.

\paragraph{Dueling architecture.} The Dueling network \citep{Wang2016} decomposes the Q-function into a state value $V(s)$ and an action advantage $A(s, a)$:
\begin{equation}
Q(s, a) = V(s) + A(s, a) - \frac{1}{|A|}\sum_{a'} A(s, a'),
\end{equation}
where $|A|$ is the number of actions. This decomposition allows the network to learn state values without evaluating every action, which helps in states where the action choice matters little.

\paragraph{Prioritized experience replay.} Uniform replay treats all transitions equally. Prioritized experience replay (PER) \citep{Schaul2016} samples transitions proportionally to their TD error magnitude, revisiting surprising transitions more often. The sampling probability of transition $i$ is:
\begin{equation}
P(i) = \frac{p_i^\alpha}{\sum_k p_k^\alpha}, \quad p_i = |\delta_i| + \epsilon_p,
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ controls prioritization strength and $\epsilon_p$ is a small constant ensuring nonzero probability. Non-uniform sampling introduces bias, corrected by importance-sampling (IS) weights:
\begin{equation}
w_i = \left(\frac{1}{N \cdot P(i)}\right)^\beta\!\bigg/\; \max_j w_j,
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the number of stored transitions and $\beta$ is annealed from a starting value to 1 during training, gradually applying the full bias correction.

\paragraph{Multi-step returns.} Instead of bootstrapping from the immediate next state, $n$-step returns \citep{Sutton1988} accumulate rewards over $n$ steps:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:nstep}
R_t^{(n)} = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \gamma^k r_{t+k+1} + \gamma^n \max_{a'} Q(s_{t+n}, a'; \theta^-).
\end{equation}
This propagates reward information faster at the cost of increased variance. Moderate values of $n$ (typically 2--5) improve learning speed in practice.

\paragraph{Rainbow.} \citet{Hessel2018} combined six DQN extensions into a single agent: Double DQN, Dueling, PER, multi-step returns, distributional RL (C51), and NoisyNet. An ablation study showed that each component contributes, with prioritized replay and multi-step returns providing the largest individual gains.

We adopt four of these six components: Double DQN, Dueling, PER, and 3-step returns. We omit distributional RL due to implementation complexity and NoisyNet because $\varepsilon$-greedy exploration is sufficient at smaller scales \citep{ObandoCeron2021, Fortunato2018}.

\subsection{Training Stability Techniques}
\label{sec:stability}

\paragraph{Polyak soft target updates.} The original DQN copies $\theta$ to $\theta^-$ every $C$ steps, creating discontinuities in the targets. Polyak averaging \citep{Polyak1992, Lillicrap2016} replaces the hard copy with an exponential moving average, updating the target at every step:
\begin{equation}
\theta^- \leftarrow (1 - \tau)\,\theta^- + \tau\,\theta,
\end{equation}
with a small $\tau$. This produces smoother target evolution and removes the need to tune $C$.

\paragraph{AdamW.} The Adam optimizer applies L2 regularization to the gradient, which interacts poorly with adaptive learning rates. AdamW \citep{Loshchilov2019} decouples weight decay from the gradient update, providing effective regularization that helps maintain network plasticity during long training runs.

\paragraph{LayerNorm.} Batch normalization is unreliable in RL because the data distribution is non-stationary and effective batch sizes are small. LayerNorm \citep{Ba2016} normalizes across features within each sample, independent of the batch, making it suitable for the single-sample updates common in RL.

\subsection{Reward Shaping}
\label{sec:reward_shaping_bg}

When multiple reward components are combined with different weights, the agent maximizes the weighted sum, which may not correspond to any single intended behavior. This kind of mismatch is commonly described as reward hacking \citep{Amodei2016}. In a survival setting, it creates a tension: a high food reward produces aggressive foraging at the cost of safety, a high death penalty produces shelter-seeking at the cost of starvation, and a high shelter reward produces passive camping. The interaction between these components, not just their individual magnitudes, determines the learned policy.

This interaction is the central object of study. We fix the agent and vary only the reward weights to isolate their effect on behavior.

Reward hacking is not, however, a property of the reward function alone. An agent exploits only the misspecifications it is capable of reaching. \citet{Pan2022} make this dependence concrete: holding misspecified rewards fixed while varying agent capability (model capacity, action-space resolution, observation noise, and training time), they find that more capable agents systematically earn higher \emph{proxy} reward and lower \emph{true} reward, in some cases crossing capability thresholds at which behavior changes abruptly. Reward hacking is, in this sense, an interaction between reward design and agent capability. We refer to such rewards informally as \emph{leaky}: their episode return can be inflated by behavior that does not serve the intended objective (survival, in this work) and may actively oppose it. This is a specific form of misspecification.

\subsection{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

\paragraph{Gridworld benchmarks for multi-objective survival.} Gridworlds have a long history as testbeds for RL phenomena that are hard to disentangle in continuous-action settings. \citet{Leike2017} introduce a suite of safety-focused gridworlds covering specification gaming, side effects, and distributional shift, several of which are reward-hacking benchmarks of the kind discussed above. \citet{ChevalierBoisvert2023} provide the modular Minigrid framework, including configurations such as MiniGrid-Lava that pose explicit progress-versus-safety trade-offs. The closest prior environment to ours is the Crafter benchmark of \citet{Hafner2022}: a procedurally-generated multi-objective survival gridworld in which the agent forages, drinks, sleeps, fights, and builds shelter. NetHack-derived environments \citep{Kuttler2020, Samvelyan2021} extend the paradigm to richer action spaces and longer horizons. We construct a custom environment rather than build on top of one of the libraries above. The decisive constraint is compute: this thesis trains every model on a laptop, and the $3 \times 2 \times 4$ ablation requires twenty-four training runs. The custom env operates on a $64 \times 64$ grid with simple integer state and produces a $678$-dimensional flat observation, so episodes step fast enough to fit $2{,}000{,}000$ training steps inside a few hours per cell. The choice also suits the research question. Crafter expands the survival theme into a $17$-action benchmark spanning biomes, crafting, inventory, tools, and twenty-two achievements: a much larger problem than the reward-shape question under a single survival objective, with most of its action space irrelevant to ours. MiniGrid is narrower but its built-in environments are explicitly goal-oriented \citep{ChevalierBoisvert2023}, framed around reaching a target tile or picking up an object. Our food-enemies-shelters-hunger-health combination with active enemy pursuit and an indefinite survival horizon therefore warranted a simpler, purely numpy-based implementation.

\paragraph{Partial observability and recurrent alternatives.} Frame stacking \citep{Mnih2015}, used here as the observation-side ablation variable, is one of two standard techniques for handling state distributions that lack motion information. The other is to replace the feed-forward Q-network with a recurrent one: \citet{Hausknecht2015} introduce DRQN, which augments DQN with an LSTM head and is the canonical recurrent alternative to frame stacking. We use frame stacking rather than DRQN because frame stacking integrates trivially with the standard replay buffer, while recurrent encoders require hidden-state continuity across replayed sequences and add training-loop complexity. The agent's relevant temporal context in this environment is short (enemy velocity over $\leq 4$ ticks), within the regime where frame stacking is empirically competitive with recurrent encoders. A direct comparison between the two is a natural follow-up.

\section{Environment}
\label{sec:environment}
% =============================================================================

Grid-based environments are a common testbed for reinforcement learning, from safety-focused gridworlds \citep{Leike2017} to the modular Minigrid framework \citep{ChevalierBoisvert2023}. Our environment extends this tradition with a multi-objective survival task that emphasizes the reward design problem.

\subsection{World Design}
\label{sec:world_design}

The environment is a $64 \times 64$ bounded grid with discrete time steps. An episode ends when the agent dies (health reaches zero) or when an episode cap on the number of ticks is reached. The choice of cap and its empirical non-bindingness are discussed in Section~\ref{sec:setup}. The world contains four entity types.

\paragraph{Agent.} A single agent with two resource bars: health $h$ and hunger $u$, both with maximum value $h_\text{max} = u_\text{max} = 100$. The agent spawns at the grid center with both bars full. Hunger depletes by $0.2$ per tick and by an additional $0.5$ when moving. When hunger reaches zero, the agent starves and loses $0.5$ health per tick. When hunger exceeds the threshold $u_\text{thresh} = 0.3$ ($30\%$ of maximum), health regenerates at $0.5$ per tick up to the cap.

\paragraph{Enemies.} Mobile entities that deal 15 damage on contact (Manhattan distance $\leq 1$). Each tick, an enemy moves with probability 0.5. Within a vision range of 3 (Manhattan distance), enemies pursue the agent along the axis of greatest distance. Outside this range, they move randomly. Enemies can't enter shelter tiles or tiles occupied by another enemy. The world supports up to 8 enemies simultaneously, with roughly a third of this capacity present at episode start. New enemies appear with probability 0.01 per tick.

\paragraph{Food.} Static items that restore 20 hunger when consumed by stepping onto their tile. Up to 36 food items exist simultaneously, with half present at episode start. New food appears with probability 0.05 per tick.

\paragraph{Shelters.} Static tiles generated at episode start with density 0.01 per cell (approximately 41 shelters per episode). While in a shelter, the agent takes no damage from enemies. Shelters don't protect against starvation.

Each episode uses a different random seed, producing a unique shelter layout and initial entity placement. Enemy and food spawning continues throughout the episode, maintaining population pressure.

\subsection{Observation Space}

The agent observes through a $15 \times 15$ window (radius $r_\text{obs} = 7$) centered on the agent. The observation consists of three binary spatial channels:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item Enemy presence
\item Food presence
\item Shelter presence
\end{enumerate}
Cells outside the grid boundary are zero. Three scalar features are appended: $\frac{h}{h_\text{max}}$, $\frac{u}{u_\text{max}}$, and a binary shelter indicator. The full single-frame observation is a $678$-dimensional vector ($3 \times 15 \times 15 = 675$ spatial entries plus $3$ scalar features). The 4-frame stacked variant introduced as the observation-side ablation in Section~\ref{sec:ablation} concatenates the spatial channels of the four most recent frames, raising the spatial channel count from $3$ to $12$. The spatial component therefore grows by an exact factor of $4$ (from $675$ to $2{,}700$), but the three scalar features are taken from the current frame only, since they reflect the cumulative state of the agent and don't benefit from temporal stacking. Thus, the total observation dimension grows from $678$ to $2{,}703$, by a factor of $2703 / 678 \approx 3.99$. Figure~\ref{fig:obs_space} illustrates the layout.

\subsection{Action Space}

The agent has five discrete actions: stay in place, or move one cell up, down, left, or right. Movement is clamped to the grid boundaries.

\subsection{Reward Structure}
\label{sec:reward_structure}

The reward at each step is a weighted sum of up to eleven components:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:reward}
\begin{aligned}
r_t ={}& w_\text{food}\, i_t^{(\text{eat})}
    + w_\text{edm}\, d_t^{(\text{e})}
    + w_\text{sdm}\, d_t^{(\text{s})}
    + w_\text{death}\, i_t^{(\text{die})} \\
& + w_\text{hunger}\, i_t^{(\text{low})}
    + w_\text{hprop}(1 - u_t)
    + w_\text{tick} \\
& + w_\text{fprox}\, \Delta\phi_t^{(\text{f})}
    + w_\text{eprox}\, \Delta\phi_t^{(\text{e})}
    + w_\text{sprox}\, \Delta\phi_t^{(\text{s})} \\
& + w_\text{shelter}\, i_t^{(\text{safe})},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $w_{\cdot} \in \mathbb{R}$ are tunable scalar weights; $i_t^{(\text{eat})}, i_t^{(\text{die})}, i_t^{(\text{low})}, i_t^{(\text{safe})} \in \{0,1\}$ are binary indicators for food eaten, death, low hunger, and shelter safety; $d_t^{(\text{e})}$ and $d_t^{(\text{s})}$ are enemy damage and starvation damage; and $u_t \in [0, 1]$ is the hunger ratio.

Table~\ref{tab:default_rewards} lists all reward components and their baseline weights.

The three proximity terms reward per-step changes in closeness to a target, rather than absolute closeness. For target type $x \in \{\text{f}, \text{e}, \text{s}\}$ (food, enemy, shelter), define the closeness score
\begin{equation}
\phi_t^{(x)} = \frac{dist_\text{max} - dist_t^{(x)} + 1}{dist_\text{max} + 1} \in [0, 1],
\end{equation}
where $dist_t^{(x)}$ is the Manhattan distance to the nearest visible entity of type $x$ and $dist_\text{max} = 2r_\text{obs}$ (maximum Manhattan distance to any cell within the observation radius). If no entity is visible, $\phi_t^{(x)} = 0$. The per-step closeness change $\Delta\phi_t^{(x)} = \phi_t^{(x)} - \phi_{t-1}^{(x)}$ is positive when the agent moved closer to the target in that step and negative when it moved away. We reward this per-step change rather than the absolute closeness because rewarding absolute closeness would also reward standing still next to a target (for example, hovering next to a food tile without eating it). The per-step change form gives zero reward for staying in place, so it only pays the agent for moving toward the target.

Two proximity terms can be conditionally gated on the hunger ratio, using a dedicated gating threshold $u_\text{gate}$. When conditional gating is enabled, the food proximity fires only when $u_t < u_\text{gate}$ (hungry) and the shelter proximity fires only when $u_t \geq u_\text{gate}$ (well-fed). This encodes a behavioral mode switch through the reward function: the agent receives a spatial gradient toward food when it needs to eat and toward shelter when it doesn't.

These components create competing pressures. Collecting food requires movement, which costs hunger and exposes the agent to enemies. Shelters neutralize enemy damage but provide no food. The hunger-proportional penalty grows every tick, creating urgency to eat that the agent can't escape through passivity.

Another design consideration is the relative magnitude of continuous per-tick rewards versus sparse event rewards. A per-tick reward of $+0.1$ accumulated over a 700-step episode produces $+70$, which can dominate a food reward of $+5.0$. This creates incentives for passive survival: the agent learns to avoid risk rather than seek food. Similarly, a food proximity reward that is too large relative to the food-eaten reward incentivizes hovering near food without eating it, since eating removes the food and terminates the proximity stream. These failure modes motivate the experimental configurations in Section~\ref{sec:experiments}.

These weights are the primary independent variable in our experiments.

\section{Agent Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}
% =============================================================================

The agent implements every technique introduced in Section~\ref{sec:background}: Double DQN, the Dueling architecture, prioritized experience replay, $n$-step returns, Polyak target updates, AdamW with decoupled weight decay, and LayerNorm. The subsections below describe the network architecture, the training procedure, and the hyperparameters used; full per-parameter values are in Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparams}.

\subsection{Network}
\label{sec:network}

The agent is implemented in PyTorch \citep{Paszke2019}. Since the present work studies reward shape and observation-space changes rather than architecture, the architecture must be adequate for the task but doesn't need to be optimal. The key requirement is sufficient representational capacity so that the architecture isn't the bottleneck. A convolutional encoder is the natural choice because the observation has spatial structure ($3k \times 15 \times 15$ grid for frame-stack size $k$). The Dueling head is inherited from the Rainbow-lite recipe. Convolutional channel counts $(32, 64, 64)$ follow the Atari-DQN backbone of \citet{Mnih2015}, inherited by subsequent DQN extensions \citep{Wang2016, Hessel2018}. The fully-connected hidden dimensions ($256$, $128$) are scaled down from the Atari $512$ to fit the smaller $678$-dimensional flat observation. They were not separately tuned for this task. For single-frame input ($k=1$), the network has $970{,}438$ trainable parameters for a $678$-dimensional input with $5$ output actions. The only architectural change in the frame-stacked cells ($k=4$) is the input depth of the first convolution. This provides ample capacity for the decision problem. Kernel sizes ($3 \times 3$ throughout) are small enough to capture useful local structure on a $15 \times 15$ grid without spanning the entire field of view. The stride profile $(1, 1, 2)$ downsamples the spatial dimension from $15$ to $7$ once at the third convolution. Two stride-2 layers would reduce it too aggressively, and three stride-1 layers would leave the spatial dimension unchanged. These and the other architectural choices (depth, width) were not separately tuned for this task and do not derive from specific references. The comparisons reported below depend on the architecture being held constant across cells, not on it being optimal.

The encoder splits the input into a $3 \times 15 \times 15$ spatial grid and 3 scalar features. Three convolutional layers process the spatial grid:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item $\text{Conv2d}(3 \to 32,\; 3 \times 3,\; \text{stride}\ 1,\; \text{pad}\ 1)$ \textrightarrow\ ReLU \textrightarrow\ LayerNorm
\item $\text{Conv2d}(32 \to 64,\; 3 \times 3,\; \text{stride}\ 1,\; \text{pad}\ 1)$ \textrightarrow\ ReLU \textrightarrow\ LayerNorm
\item $\text{Conv2d}(64 \to 64,\; 3 \times 3,\; \text{stride}\ 2,\; \text{pad}\ 0)$ \textrightarrow\ ReLU
\end{enumerate}
The third layer reduces the spatial dimensions from $15 \times 15$ to $7 \times 7$, producing a 3136-dimensional feature vector. This is concatenated with the 3 scalar features and projected through a fully connected layer ($3139 \to 256$) with ReLU and LayerNorm.

The Dueling head splits the 256-dimensional representation into two streams. The value stream maps $256 \to 128 \to 1$. The advantage stream maps $256 \to 128 \to 5$. Q-values are computed as $Q(s,a) = V(s) + A(s,a) - \bar{A}(s)$.

\subsection{Training Algorithm}
\label{sec:training_algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:training} summarizes the training procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Rainbow-lite DQN training.}
\label{alg:training}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Initialize policy network $\theta$, target network $\theta^- \leftarrow \theta$
\State Initialize prioritized replay buffer $\mathcal{D}$, $n$-step accumulator
\State Collect 10{,}000 transitions with random policy into $\mathcal{D}$
\For{$t = 1, \ldots, T$}
    \State Select $a_t$ via $\varepsilon$-greedy from $Q(\cdot; \theta)$, decay $\varepsilon$
    \State Execute $a_t$, observe $r_t, s_{t+1}, d_t$
    \State Compute $n$-step return; commit to $\mathcal{D}$ when ready
    \State Sample batch from $\mathcal{D}$ with PER; compute importance-sampling (IS) weights $w_i$
    \State $y_i = R_i^{(n)} + (1 - d_i)\,\gamma^n\, Q\!\left(s_i',\, \arg\max_{a'} Q(s_i', a'; \theta);\, \theta^-\right)$
    \State $L = \frac{1}{B}\sum_i w_i \cdot \text{Huber}(Q(s_i, a_i; \theta),\; y_i)$
    \State Update $\theta$ via AdamW with gradient clipping
    \State $\theta^- \leftarrow (1-\tau)\,\theta^- + \tau\,\theta$
    \State Update priorities in $\mathcal{D}$ from new TD errors
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
Here, $d_i \in \{0,1\}$ indicates whether sampled transition $i$ is terminal, and $B$ denotes the batch size.
\paragraph{Experience collection.} At each step, the transition $(s_t, a_t, r_t, s_{t+1}, d_t)$ enters an $n$-step accumulator. Once $n = 3$ transitions have accumulated, the oldest is committed to the replay buffer with return $R_t^{(3)} = r_t + \gamma r_{t+1} + \gamma^2 r_{t+2}$, bootstrap state $s_{t+3}$, and discount factor $\gamma^3$. At episode termination (agent death), remaining entries are flushed with shortened returns. On episode truncation (time limit), pending entries are discarded to prevent cross-episode contamination of $n$-step returns.

The distinction between termination and truncation matters for the bootstrap target. A terminated episode (death) sets $d = 1$, zeroing the bootstrap term. A truncated episode sets $d = 0$, allowing bootstrapping from the final state since the agent was still alive.

\paragraph{Replay buffer.} Transitions are stored in pre-allocated NumPy arrays \citep{Harris2020}. A SumTree data structure \citep{Schaul2016} supports efficient ($O(\log n)$) priority updates and proportional sampling. Stratified sampling divides $[0, P_\text{total}]$ into equal segments and draws one sample per segment, reducing variance compared to pure proportional sampling.

\paragraph{Optimization.} AdamW with decoupled weight decay ($10^{-4}$) on convolutional and linear weights. Bias and normalization parameters are exempt. Gradients are clipped to norm 1.0. The learning rate is held constant.

\paragraph{Exploration.} $\varepsilon$-greedy with linear decay from 1.0 to 0.01 over the first 500{,}000 steps (one quarter of training), then held constant. The non-zero floor preserves a small amount of exploration throughout the remaining training steps.

\subsection{Hyperparameters}
\label{sec:hyperparameters}

Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparams} lists all hyperparameters. Values follow established defaults from the literature rather than environment-specific tuning. The frame-stack size $k$ is the observation-side ablation variable (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}). Every other hyperparameter is held constant across all twenty-four ablation runs.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

Each ablation run trains for $T = 2{,}000{,}000$ timesteps (a few hours on consumer GPUs). The misspecified configurations (\textsc{baseline}, \textsc{absolute\_proximity}, and their frame-stacked variants) cap episodes at $1{,}000$ ticks. The \textsc{minimal} configurations cap at $50{,}000$ ticks. An episode cap is required by the protocol: a policy could in principle survive indefinitely, which would prevent benchmark and training termination. We initially used a $1{,}000$-tick cap throughout, chosen to leave roughly $30\%$ headroom above the heuristic's mean survival of $\approx 769$ ticks. Preliminary results showed that the misspecified configurations reached the cap in only a negligible fraction of episodes, because their reward-hacking failure modes terminate episodes well before the cap. We accordingly left their cap at $1{,}000$. The \textsc{minimal} configurations, in contrast, reached the cap in a non-negligible fraction of episodes (the minimal reward is not intentionally misspecified, so the agent survives long enough for right-censoring at the lower cap to materially affect the cell mean), so we raised their cap to $50{,}000$ ticks. The heuristic baseline is benchmarked at both caps so that each cell is paired with a heuristic measurement at the same cap. The heuristic's mean survival is essentially unchanged between the two caps ($768.8$ at cap $1{,}000$ vs $772.1$ at cap $50{,}000$). Greedy in-training evaluation runs every $5{,}000$ steps with $100$ episodes ($\varepsilon = 0$). Final benchmarks evaluate the best in-training checkpoint over $100$ episodes with fixed seeds ($1000$--$1099$). We report mean survival ticks (with $95\%$ confidence interval), mean food eaten, mean damage taken, and death rate. Each cell is run with four random seeds ($42$, $43$, $44$, $45$). The agent architecture, all hyperparameters in Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparams}, and the training budget are held constant. The only variables are the reward configuration (Section~\ref{sec:reward_configs}) and the frame-stack size $k \in \{1, 4\}$ (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}).

\paragraph{Heuristic baseline.} A scripted agent provides a non-learned reference. The heuristic camps in the nearest shelter and leaves only when the hunger ratio drops below $0.5$, at which point it moves toward the nearest food. It flees from enemies within Manhattan distance $5$. Over $100$ evaluation episodes, this agent survives a mean of $769 \pm 19$ ticks ($95\%$ CI), eats $1.19$ food per episode, and dies in $94\%$ of episodes. The remaining $6\%$ reach the $1{,}000$-tick cap. The heuristic is rerun on the same evaluation seeds for every cell, so heuristic and DQN episodes are matched by evaluation world. Each cell mean is reported with a standard error taken across its four per-seed means. For each cell, we compute one number per world by averaging across its four training seeds, giving $100$ per-world cell values. Subtracting the heuristic's survival on each world gives $100$ differences. We report the mean and $95\%$ confidence interval of those differences as the primary statement, with a two-sided $p$-value alongside.

\subsection{Failure Modes}
\label{sec:failure_modes}

Early experiments revealed three degenerate policies that emerged reliably under certain reward configurations. These modes aren't mutually exclusive: all three appeared within a single baseline training run at different stages and in different episodes, with the agent alternating between hovering near food, seeking enemies, and oscillating in place depending on the local state distribution.

\paragraph{Oscillation.} The agent remains passive or oscillates between adjacent cells. This behavior was observed under several reward configurations and its precise cause requires further investigation.

\paragraph{Food hovering.} The agent moves near food but doesn't eat it. This occurs when the proximity reward exceeds the hunger penalty over the same interval. Under absolute closeness ($\phi$ rather than $\Delta\phi$), an agent $5$ cells from food with $w_\text{fprox} = 0.5$ earns $w_\text{fprox} \cdot \phi = 0.5 \cdot 10/15 \approx +0.33$ per tick from proximity, while the average hunger penalty is $-0.15$ per tick (taking $w_\text{hprop} = -0.3$ at average hunger ratio $0.5$). The net reward for inaction is positive. Eating the food would remove the proximity stream. Avoiding this failure mode requires keeping the per-tick proximity reward below the per-tick hunger penalty, which under absolute closeness translates to $w_\text{fprox} < |w_\text{hprop}|/2$.

\paragraph{Suicide.} When the proximity gradient is too weak to guide the agent toward food, the agent can't learn to forage. Without foraging, every tick accumulates hunger-proportional penalty. A stationary agent surviving 700 ticks until starvation accumulates approximately $-135$ from $w_\text{hprop}$ and $-100$ from $w_\text{sdm}$, totaling $-235$ before the death penalty. The agent discovers that dying quickly is preferable: seeking enemies yields a fast death at total cost $\approx -24$ (a few enemy hits plus the death penalty), roughly 10$\times$ less than slow starvation. This is a rational response to a reward function where living is more expensive than dying.

The food hovering and suicide failure modes are clear instances of reward hacking~\citep{Amodei2016, Leike2017}: the agent optimizes the reward function as specified, but the resulting behavior diverges from the designer's intent. The cause of oscillation is less certain and requires further investigation. These failure modes motivate the reward configurations introduced in Section~\ref{sec:reward_configs}. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports a five-category behavioral breakdown that maps onto them only approximately: the suicide mode corresponds directly to the \textit{enemy suicide} category, while oscillation and food hovering both fall under \textit{exposed stasis} or \textit{forage/die} depending on how much food the agent ate.

\subsection{Reward Configurations}
\label{sec:reward_configs}

The failure modes above show that poorly chosen reward weights produce degenerate policies. Drawing on this analysis, we test four reward configurations: \textsc{baseline} as the reference, \textsc{absolute\_proximity} which alters the form of the proximity term, \textsc{minimal} which strips the reward to its directional gradients, and \textsc{weak\_proximity} as a single-seed illustration of what happens when the foraging signal is too weak. Table~\ref{tab:reward_configs} summarizes them; each is motivated in turn below.

\paragraph{Baseline.} The default weights (Table~\ref{tab:default_rewards}). The hunger-proportional penalty ($-0.3$) creates urgency, the food proximity gradient ($+0.1$) provides a directional signal, and the food, damage, shelter, and death components encode the rest of the survival objective. The configuration is dense and compositional: every entity type contributes a reward signal and every internal state has a corresponding term. This makes \textsc{baseline} the most informative reward in principle, but also the most exposed to interactions between components.

\paragraph{Absolute proximity.} The proximity terms are switched from per-step closeness change $\Delta\phi$ to absolute closeness $\phi$. Every other weight matches \textsc{baseline}. This configuration tests the importance of the closeness-change reward shape. Under $\Delta\phi$, the cumulative proximity reward over a sequence of steps equals $w_\text{fprox} \cdot (\phi_\text{final} - \phi_\text{initial})$, bounded by $w_\text{fprox}$ regardless of how many steps the agent takes: a round-trip back to the starting position earns zero, and the only way to earn proximity reward is to end the trajectory closer to a target than where it started. Under $\phi$, a stationary agent at distance $d$ from food earns $w_\text{fprox} \cdot \phi(d)$ per tick indefinitely, so cumulative proximity reward grows linearly with time spent near food, without bound. With the default $w_\text{fprox} = 0.1$ the per-tick proximity reward ($\leq 0.1$) still falls below the average hunger penalty ($0.15$), so hovering is not strictly net-positive per tick; but the gradient signal is qualitatively different from \textsc{baseline}, and that difference is what this cell isolates.

\paragraph{Minimal.} A minimal reward set with only three closeness-change proximity gradients and the death penalty:
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=1.6em]
  \item food-proximity ($w_\text{fprox}=+0.15$), gated to fire only when $u_t < 0.5$ (hungry),
  \item shelter-proximity ($w_\text{sprox}=+0.15$), gated to fire only when $u_t \geq 0.5$ (well-fed),
  \item enemy-proximity ($w_\text{eprox}=-0.5$), always active,
  \item death ($w_\text{death}=-10$).
\end{itemize}
Every other weight is set to zero. The motivation is to cleanly separate the movement-based rewards from the event-driven rewards (food eaten, damage taken, shelter safety) and from the always-on hunger penalty: each of the remaining three signals corresponds to a single objective, and the gating mirrors the heuristic's mode switch (forage when hungry, shelter when not). Setting $w_\text{hprop}$ and $w_\text{sdm}$ to zero also removes the suicide pressure described in Section~\ref{sec:failure_modes}: with both terms zeroed, slow starvation and fast death cost the same terminal $-10$, so the difference in accumulated penalty between the two strategies that incentivizes a randomly-initialized policy to die quickly disappears. \paragraph{Weak proximity (illustration).} The rewards are the same as in \textsc{baseline}, but the food proximity weight is reduced from $0.1$ to $0.02$. At this weight, one step toward food earns approximately $0.02 / 15 \approx 0.00133$ in proximity reward (a single cell of approach is $1/15$ of the closeness range, from Section~\ref{sec:reward_structure}), which is smaller than the extra hunger penalty that move imposes on every future tick ($|w_\text{hprop}| \cdot 0.5 / u_\text{max} = 0.0015$ per tick, from the $0.5$ extra hunger drop each move costs). At random initialization, before the agent has learned that approaching food leads to eating it, the expected reward from moving toward food does not outweigh this hunger cost, and the agent prefers to die quickly rather than slowly starve, which is the suicide failure mode of Section~\ref{sec:failure_modes}. This is included as a single-seed illustration, not as part of the main ablation. Section~\ref{sec:weak_prox} reports the resulting agent behavior qualitatively.

\subsection{Weak Proximity: a Single-Seed Illustration}
\label{sec:weak_prox}

At random initialization, before the agent has learned that approaching food leads to eating it, the expected reward from moving toward food does not outweigh the extra hunger cost from movement (Section~\ref{sec:reward_configs}). The agent should therefore prefer to die quickly rather than slowly starve. We trained a single seed of \textsc{weak\_proximity} ($w_\text{fprox}=0.02$, all other weights matching \textsc{baseline}) for two million steps and benchmarked the result over $100$ episodes. The agent reached mean survival $647 \pm 33$ ticks with $0.66$ food per episode and a $99\%$ death rate. These aggregate statistics match the four-seed \textsc{baseline} cell mean ($647$ ticks, $0.66$ food, $98\%$ death rate; Table~\ref{tab:cell_results}) to the precision reported. The myopic argument is therefore a necessary condition on the proximity weight, not a predictor of long-run behavior: random exploration during early training ($\varepsilon$-greedy with $\varepsilon$ starting at $1.0$) bumps the agent into food often enough to discover the $+5$ eating reward by accident, after which the agent learns to forage regardless of the local gradient's size. We don't draw conclusions from this single seed beyond that observation.

\subsection{Reward $\times$ Observation Ablation}
\label{sec:ablation}

The main experiment crosses three reward configurations (\textsc{baseline}, \textsc{absolute\_proximity}, \textsc{minimal}) with two observation types (single-frame, $k = 1$, and 4-frame stacked, $k = 4$), four random seeds per cell. Each run trains for two million timesteps under the hyperparameters in Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparams}. The only varying parameters are the reward weights (Section~\ref{sec:reward_configs}) and $k$. The benchmark protocol is described in Section~\ref{sec:setup}.

\paragraph{Predictions.} Three expectations motivated the experiments, labelled P1--P3:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(P\arabic*),nosep,leftmargin=2.8em]
  \item \textsc{minimal} with frame stacking is the highest-performing of the six cells.
  \item \textsc{minimal} with frame stacking exceeds the heuristic.
  \item Frame stacking improves every reward configuration over its single-frame counterpart.
\end{enumerate}
The reasoning behind P1 and P2 was that the cleanest reward (\textsc{minimal}) combined with the temporal context required to infer enemy velocity (4-frame stacking) should produce the strongest policy in the experiment, and that this strongest policy would be sufficient to beat the scripted heuristic. P3 captured the prior expectation that adding observation history is at worst neutral: extra information that an RL agent can in principle ignore shouldn't degrade learned performance. These three predictions are what the ablation was designed to test. As Section~\ref{sec:analysis} reports, the experiment held P1 and refuted both P2 and P3. It also revealed a structured effect that none of the three predictions anticipated.

\subsection{Summary of Observations}
\label{sec:analysis}

We organize the analysis around the three predictions P1--P3.

\paragraph{(P1) \textsc{minimal\_fs} is the highest-performing cell. HELD.}
Mean survival across the six cells ranges from $555$ (\textsc{baseline\_fs}) to $761$ (\textsc{minimal\_fs}). \textsc{minimal\_fs} is the only cell that comes close to the heuristic. The second-best cell is \textsc{minimal} (single-frame) at $686$, trailing by $75$ ticks. The within-cell standard error of \textsc{minimal\_fs} is small enough that no other cell is consistent with matching its mean.

\paragraph{(P2) \textsc{minimal\_fs} exceeds the heuristic. NOT HELD.}
Frame stacking lifts \textsc{minimal} from a mean of $686$ to $761$ ticks, a $+75$-tick gain. The paired gap to the heuristic is $-10.7$ ticks ($95\%$ CI $[-36, +15]$, $p = 0.41$): the CI includes zero, so \textsc{minimal\_fs} could be tied with the heuristic, but we cannot show it exceeds the heuristic. We had expected a clear excess and didn't get one.

\paragraph{(P3) Frame stacking improves every reward configuration. NOT HELD.}
With $\Delta = \text{mean}(\text{fs}) - \text{mean}(\text{sf})$: $\Delta = +75$ for \textsc{minimal}, $\Delta = -47$ for \textsc{absolute\_proximity}, $\Delta = -92$ for \textsc{baseline} (Figure~\ref{fig:ablation_grid}, third panel). Frame stacking helps the cleanest reward, but hurts performance for both leakier ones. The direction reverses with reward shape. This is the largest departure from our prior expectation, plausible mechanisms are discussed in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\paragraph{Single-frame cells cluster well below the heuristic.}
Mean survival across the three single-frame reward configurations is $686$ for \textsc{minimal}, $647$ for \textsc{baseline}, and $634$ for \textsc{absolute\_proximity}, against the heuristic's $769$. Every single-frame seed is significantly outperformed by the heuristic at $p < 0.001$. The reward configuration matters at the margin (range $\approx 40$ ticks across the three rewards) but not enough to bring DQN within reach of the heuristic without temporal context.

\paragraph{Foraging over Safety.}
Frame stacking changes the foraging rate substantially: from $0.15$ to $2.33$ for \textsc{minimal}, from $0.43$ to $4.81$ for \textsc{absolute\_proximity}, and from $0.66$ to $4.83$ for \textsc{baseline}. The frame-stacked reward gradients teach the agent to seek food more aggressively, but in the leaky reward configurations (\textsc{baseline}, \textsc{absolute\_proximity}) the resulting policy spends more ticks exposed to enemies and dies sooner. Foraging behavior is acquired. Safety behavior is not. Only \textsc{minimal\_fs} reaches a foraging rate consistent with sustained survival ($2.33$ food per episode at survival within $11$ ticks of the heuristic, against the heuristic's $1.19$ food at $769$ ticks).

\paragraph{Shelters are not used.}
Section~\ref{sec:failure_modes} reports that DQN cells reach shelter occupancy under $1.5\%$ of survived ticks regardless of reward configuration, against the heuristic's $82\%$. For the \textsc{minimal} configuration this is informative because the reward was specifically designed to teach the heuristic's shelter mode via a shelter-proximity gradient gated on hunger $\geq u_\text{gate}$ (Section~\ref{sec:reward_configs}). One plausible hypothesis is that the gate never opens: the agent's hunger stays below $u_\text{gate} = 0.5$ for most of the episode, so the shelter-proximity reward never fires.

To test this, we logged the agent's hunger ratio at every tick and ran the four trained \textsc{minimal\_fs} checkpoints on the $100$-world evaluation suite. Across these $400$ episodes the agent's hunger was at or above $u_\text{gate}$ for a mean of $32.3\%$ of episode ticks (longest continuous open-gate streak averaged $238$ ticks per episode, median $247$, max $334$). All $400$ episodes started above the gate, since the agent begins every episode at full hunger. The gate is therefore open often enough and for long enough that the shelter-proximity reward has substantial opportunity to fire. The hypothesis as originally posed is rejected.

A second hypothesis is that the $\Delta\phi$ formulation of the shelter-proximity reward fails to teach shelter occupancy because it pays only for approaching a shelter, not for occupying one. Once the agent is inside a shelter the closeness no longer changes, so $\Delta\phi^{(s)} = 0$ on every subsequent tick. The per-tick shelter-safety reward $w_\text{shelter}$, which would directly pay for occupancy, is set to zero in the present \textsc{minimal} configuration (Table~\ref{tab:minimal_config}). The agent therefore receives no signal to remain in a shelter even if it enters one. Verifying this hypothesis would require reactivating $w_\text{shelter}$ and observing whether shelter occupancy rises. We note this as a follow-up in Section~\ref{sec:future_work}.

It is also worth noting that shelter use may not be strictly necessary for survival in this environment, since enemy density and pursuit rules of Section~\ref{sec:world_design} may simply not impose enough damage pressure to make shelter-camping a required strategy. The fact that \textsc{minimal\_fs} reaches $761$ ticks, a performance similar to the heuristic's, without shelter use supports this possibility.

\paragraph{No DQN cell beats the heuristic.}
The closest cell, \textsc{minimal\_fs}, reaches the heuristic within statistical noise ($95\%$ CI for the gap $[-36, +15]$). The other five cells fall by $86$ to $214$ ticks, with confidence intervals well below zero. The single-seed \textsc{weak\_proximity} illustration (Section~\ref{sec:weak_prox}) is also below the heuristic.

\paragraph{Closeness-change vs absolute closeness.}
The contrast between \textsc{baseline} and \textsc{absolute\_proximity} isolates the effect of the closeness-change reward shape. At single-frame, switching the proximity term from $\Delta\phi$ to $\phi$ degrades survival by $13$ ticks ($647 \to 634$) and lowers food intake from $0.66$ to $0.43$ per episode. The lower food intake is consistent with the agent earning proximity reward for being near food without crossing onto the food tile to eat it. At frame-stacked, however, the same switch improves survival by $32$ ticks ($555 \to 587$) with essentially unchanged food intake ($4.83 \to 4.81$). The 4-frame stack appears to give the agent enough temporal information to navigate onto food rather than hover next to it, so the absolute form is no worse than the closeness-change form at this observation level. The closeness-change form, used in \textsc{minimal}, remains the only reward shape under which frame stacking improves survival relative to its own single-frame counterpart.

% =============================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% =============================================================================

\paragraph{What we set out to test, and what we found.}
We began with a specific, optimistic prediction (P1 and P2): that frame stacking applied to a clean minimal reward (\textsc{minimal}) would lift a DQN agent above the scripted heuristic. The data partially support it. \textsc{minimal\_fs} is the best-performing cell, by a clear margin over every other reward $\times$ observation combination, and frame stacking does add $+75$ ticks of mean survival on this clean reward. The paired gap to the heuristic is $-10.7$ ticks ($95\%$ CI $[-36, +15]$, $p = 0.41$): the CI includes zero, so \textsc{minimal\_fs} could be tied with the heuristic, but we cannot show it exceeds the heuristic.

The original aim of the project was to raise DQN survival to or beyond the heuristic's level. The data are consistent with that aim being met within statistical noise (gap $-10.7$ ticks, $95\%$ CI $[-36, +15]$, not significantly different from zero) but do not affirm a clear excess. The more directly informative outcome is the reward-dependent direction of frame stacking's effect, which the rest of this section discusses. A clear result of DQN exceeding the heuristic would have confirmed the default expectation that observation history is always helpful. The direction-flip we observe contradicts that expectation: under two of three reward configurations, additional observation history hurt survival. The direction-flip is an exploratory finding, not a confirmed hypothesis.

\paragraph{Frame stacking's reward-dependent direction.}
Concretely, frame stacking degrades survival in two of three reward configurations. With the cleanest reward (\textsc{minimal}) it helps by $75$ ticks on average. With the leaky reward (\textsc{absolute\_proximity}) it hurts by $47$ ticks, while with \textsc{baseline} it hurts by $92$ ticks. In principle, additional observation channels should weakly improve the agent: extra information can be ignored if it isn't useful. The data are inconsistent with this expectation.

The obvious explanation is capacity. The observation grows $3.99\times$ while the network is held fixed, so the frame-stacked agent could simply be under-provisioned. But that explanation is reward-independent. It predicts uniform degradation, whereas the observed effect flips sign with reward shape: \textsc{minimal\_fs} is the strongest cell in the study. The effect of frame stacking is mediated by the reward, not the architecture.

Episode return clarifies the mechanism. Within each reward configuration the reward function is identical across $k$, so episode return is directly comparable across observation types. Frame stacking raises mean return on both leaky rewards (by about $+38$ on \textsc{baseline} and $+17$ on \textsc{absolute\_proximity}) while lowering survival. On \textsc{minimal} the return is essentially unchanged (it sits near $-10$ regardless of $k$, because the minimal reward is closeness-change proximity gradients plus a terminal death penalty, neither of which scales with episode length) and survival rises. Under the leaky rewards, the frame-stacked agent is more effective at collecting reward than the single-frame agent, but at the cost of survival. The reward function is misaligned with survival, and the agent is faithfully optimizing it. This is the pattern of capability-driven reward hacking \citep{Pan2022}.

This places the direction-flip within the reward-design $\times$ capability interaction of Section~\ref{sec:reward_shaping_bg}. Frame stacking is a capability increase: it gives the agent the temporal context to forage effectively. When the reward contains exploitable surplus (the per-food bounty in \textsc{baseline}, the per-tick proximity payment in \textsc{absolute\_proximity}), the additional capability from frame stacking is spent collecting that surplus rather than improving survival. When the reward contains no such surplus (\textsc{minimal}, whose return is dominated by the terminal death penalty and whose proximity terms contribute a small bounded total), the same capability is spent on survival instead. The single-frame agents do not show this split (Section~\ref{sec:analysis}, ``single-frame performance is largely independent of reward shape'') because they are too weak to exploit any of the three rewards. Frame stacking exposes the exploitable surplus in the leakier reward designs.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=1.6em]
  \item \emph{Single environment.} Our findings are only observed in this specific environment and a single task. The environment is also custom-made rather than a standard benchmark, so the results are not directly comparable to numbers reported for Crafter, MiniGrid, or other gridworld libraries. To generalize, replication on additional environments would be necessary.
  \item \emph{Architecture held fixed.} We don't run a network-architecture sweep. The reward evidence in Section~\ref{sec:discussion} rules out an undersized $k=4$ network as the driver of the leaky-cell degradation (the effect flips sign with reward shape, and the leaky cells gain reward rather than lose it, neither of which an under-provisioned agent would do), but training the $k=4$ cells with a network scaled up to its larger input would confirm this more directly.
  \item \emph{Training budget.} Due to limited computational budget, each cell was only trained for two million steps. Longer training could test whether certain configurations achieve better performance.
  \item \emph{Low seed count.} Each ablation cell uses four training seeds, below the five to ten typically recommended in RL methodology work \citep{Henderson2018, Colas2018}. The within-cell standard errors in Table~\ref{tab:cell_results} are computed from this small sample, so they themselves carry substantial estimation noise. The paired test against the heuristic averages over seeds and reaches $n = 100$ at the world level, which is statistically strong, but it does not characterize the underlying seed-to-seed variation. A larger seed budget would both tighten the cell-level uncertainty intervals and allow a direct check of how stable the frame-stacking deltas are across reseed.
  \item \emph{Single seed for the weak-proximity illustration.} Due to \textsc{weak\_proximity} being trained on only one seed, we don't draw conclusions from it beyond the qualitative note in Section~\ref{sec:weak_prox}.
\end{itemize}

% =============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% =============================================================================

We ran a $3 \times 2 \times 4$ ablation that crossed three reward configurations (\textsc{baseline}, \textsc{absolute\_proximity}, \textsc{minimal}) with two observation types (single-frame and 4-frame stacked), four random seeds per cell. We added a single-seed illustrative run of \textsc{weak\_proximity} to demonstrate the suicide failure mode under a too-small proximity weight.

\paragraph{What we predicted, what we observed.}
We committed to three predictions: that \textsc{minimal\_fs} would be the best-performing cell (P1), that it would exceed the heuristic (P2), and that frame stacking would improve every reward configuration over its single-frame counterpart (P3). P1 held: \textsc{minimal\_fs} reaches mean survival $761$ ticks, the highest of any cell. P2 is not rejected but also not affirmed: the paired gap to the heuristic is $-10.7$ ticks ($95\%$ CI $[-36, +15]$, $p = 0.41$), so \textsc{minimal\_fs} could be tied with the heuristic but we cannot show it exceeds the heuristic. P3 did not hold: frame stacking improves \textsc{minimal} ($+75$ ticks) but degrades \textsc{absolute\_proximity} ($-47$) and \textsc{baseline} ($-92$). The qualitative direction of our prior expectation holds: clean reward and temporal context together produce the best policy. The strongest claims about magnitude and universality do not.

\paragraph{The reward-dependent direction.}
Beyond refuting P3, the data reveal a directional structure to frame stacking's effect: it improves the cleanest reward configuration and degrades both leakier ones, by margins much larger than within-cell variance. We did not set out to test this. It emerged from the ablation, and is the most directly informative result of the work. Adding observation history is therefore not an unconditional improvement on this task. Under the two leakier reward designs studied here, it actively hurt the resulting agent. Plausible mechanisms are discussed in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\paragraph{Position of the contribution.}
This work studies the reward $\times$ observation interaction in DQN on one specific environment. The main contribution is a $3 \times 2 \times 4$ reward $\times$ observation $\times$ seed ablation that tests three pre-specified predictions and reveals an unexpected, but robust finding: the sign of the frame-stacking effect depends on reward shape.

\paragraph{Future work.}
\label{sec:future_work}
Four directions follow naturally from these results. First, replicate the ablation on a second multi-objective environment (for example a forked MiniGrid or Crafter setup) to test the generality of the reward-dependent direction. Second, train the $k=4$ cells with a network scaled up to match the larger input. The current architecture is the same for $k=1$ and $k=4$, despite the $k=4$ observation being roughly four times the size of the $k=1$ observation. Scaling up the $k=4$ DQN agent to match the input size would help confirm that reward design, not missing capacity, explains the decrease in survival. Third, train the frame-stacked leaky-reward cells for substantially longer than two million steps to test for further survival improvements. Fourth, test whether reactivating the per-tick shelter-safety reward ($w_\text{shelter}$, currently zero in \textsc{minimal}) produces learned shelter use. A positive result would corroborate the hypothesis in Section~\ref{sec:results} that the $\Delta\phi$ formulation's failure to reward occupancy explains why \textsc{minimal\_fs} does not adopt the heuristic's shelter-camping strategy.
