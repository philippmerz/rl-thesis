\title{When Reward Shaping Suffices and When It Does Not:\\A Bottleneck Analysis in Multi-Objective Survival Reinforcement Learning}
\author{Philipp Merz}
\date{2026}

\begin{document}
\maketitle
\begin{abstract}
We investigate what it takes to make a DQN agent consistently outperform a scripted heuristic in a multi-objective grid-based survival task, where an agent on a $64 \times 64$ grid must eat food, evade enemies, and use shelters. We organize the investigation as a sequential bottleneck analysis: we iteratively identify what is limiting the agent's performance and test the most principled intervention. Three bottlenecks emerge. First, reward design: we derive four per-step design heuristics from environment mechanics, characterize three reward-hacking failure modes, and show that even exhaustively tuned reward configurations saturate below the heuristic at $706$ mean survival (vs $768$). Second, observation: adding 4-frame temporal context without changing the reward lifts best-seed survival to $830$ ticks ($p = 0.03$), but the resulting policy collapses as epsilon and learning rate decay together. Third, training dynamics: replacing the decaying learning rate with a constant one yields the most robust result, with all three seeds significantly beating the heuristic at $830$--$836$ mean survival. Layered interventions (cyclical exploration, head resets, stronger foraging signals) produce higher single-seed peaks (best-seed mean $905$ for head resets, $944$ for resets combined with stronger signals) but with markedly higher seed-to-seed variance; individual seeds regress below the heuristic in several of these configurations. The longest single episode across all experiments reaches $2{,}477$ ticks, but no seed achieves indefinite survival. The progression validates a staged diagnostic approach to applying RL in multi-objective domains where no single intervention is sufficient, and highlights a consistency--peak-performance trade-off among the dynamics interventions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% =============================================================================

Reinforcement learning (RL) has produced agents that match or exceed human performance on complex tasks, from Atari games \citep{Mnih2015} to board games and robotic control. These results typically require large-scale compute and highly-tuned setups. A less studied but practically central question is what to do when an RL agent fails to learn an adequate policy: which part of the pipeline is the bottleneck, and what principled intervention addresses it?

Reward design is the usual first suspect. Potential-based reward shaping (PBRS) \citep{Ng1999} can accelerate learning under a sparse objective by adding a dense shaping reward derived from a state potential $\Phi(s)$, with the guarantee that the optimal policy is preserved. In practice, however, PBRS sees limited use: its guarantee breaks down under function approximation, designing $\Phi(s)$ requires knowing which states represent progress, difficult in open-ended survival tasks without clear goal states, and the framework restricts all shaping to a single scalar function of state, while practitioners often need event-driven signals that are more naturally expressed as weighted reward components.

In multi-objective settings, practitioners typically want to \emph{steer} behavior toward a specific balance of competing goals, for instance favoring aggressive foraging over cautious shelter use, or vice versa. This means deliberately defining reward functions that encode different objectives, not preserving a single one. The resulting reward functions combine multiple weighted components whose relative magnitudes directly determine what the agent optimizes. Small changes in these weights can produce qualitatively different behaviors, and there is no single ``correct'' weighting; the choice reflects a design decision about what behavior is desirable.

\paragraph{Scope and claim.} This work investigates a single concrete target, namely to make a DQN agent consistently match or exceed a scripted heuristic baseline on a multi-objective grid-survival task, and uses that target to structure a sequential bottleneck analysis. We start from reward shaping, run it to saturation, diagnose the resulting bottleneck, apply the most principled intervention, and repeat. We arrive at the following three-stage claim, each stage of which is supported by direct experiments in Section~\ref{sec:experiments}:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=1.6em]
  \item \emph{Reward shaping is necessary but saturates}. We derive four per-step design heuristics from environment mechanics (Section~\ref{sec:reward_configs}), document three reward-hacking failure modes, and systematically test the resulting reward landscape. A carefully engineered minimal reward produces the best reward-only agent (706 survival), but no reward configuration matches the heuristic (768 survival).
  \item \emph{The next bottleneck is perceptual}. A single 4-frame stack of the spatial observation, with the reward function held constant, lifts the best-seed mean survival to $830$ ($p = 0.03$) and roughly triples the agent's foraging rate. The reward signal was adequate; the agent lacked the temporal information to exploit it.
  \item \emph{The next bottleneck is training dynamics}. The frame-stacked policy peaks early and then collapses as exploration and learning rate decay together, a failure mode consistent with recent work on primacy bias and plasticity loss \citep{Nikishin2022, Dohare2024}. Replacing the OneCycle learning rate schedule with a constant rate is the most robust fix: all three seeds cluster at $830$--$836$ mean survival and significantly beat the heuristic. Layered interventions --- cyclical exploration, periodic head resets \citep{Nikishin2022}, and stronger foraging signals --- push the best-seed peak higher (up to $944$ for the full stack, with longest episode $2{,}477$ ticks) but at the cost of high seed variance, with one or two seeds regressing below the heuristic in these configurations.
\end{enumerate}

Figure~\ref{fig:bottleneck_progression} summarizes the progression. Each stage's intervention is necessary to reach the next stage's result; none of them individually is sufficient. The contribution is not a new RL algorithm but a worked example of how to diagnose what is bottlenecking a DQN agent in a multi-objective task where reward shaping alone does not suffice.

\paragraph{Agent and compute.} The agent is a Rainbow-lite DQN \citep{Hessel2018} combining Double DQN, a Dueling architecture, prioritized experience replay, and 3-step returns, along with several stability techniques (LayerNorm, AdamW, Polyak target updates). Training runs on single consumer hardware, following work by \citet{ObandoCeron2021}, \citet{Schwarzer2023}, \citet{ObandoCeron2024}, and \citet{Clark2024} demonstrating that algorithmic choices can substitute for massive compute. The architecture is held fixed across reward shaping experiments (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}) and instrumented with a single 4-frame stacking wrapper for the observation-space and dynamics experiments (Sections~\ref{sec:temporal} and \ref{sec:plasticity}).

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning Fundamentals}

We formulate the task as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) defined by a tuple $(S, A, P, R, \gamma)$: state space $S$, action space $A$, transition function $P(s' \mid s, a)$, reward function $R(s, a, s')$, and discount factor $\gamma \in [0, 1)$ \citep{SuttonBarto2018}.

The agent interacts with the environment in discrete time steps $t = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$, receiving reward $r_t \in \mathbb{R}$ at each step. The goal is to find a policy $\pi: S \to A$ that maximizes the expected discounted return $G_t = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k r_{t+k+1}$. The action-value function
\begin{equation}
Q^\pi(s, a) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[G_t \mid s_t = s,\; a_t = a\right]
\end{equation}
gives the expected return of taking action $a$ in state $s$ and following $\pi$ thereafter. The optimal action-value function $Q^*(s, a) = \max_\pi Q^\pi(s, a)$ satisfies the Bellman optimality equation:
\begin{equation}
Q^*(s, a) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[r_t + \gamma \max_{a'\in A} Q^*(s_{t+1}, a')\right],
\end{equation}
where $s_{t+1}$ is the successor state reached after taking $a$ in $s$.

Q-learning estimates $Q^*$ directly through temporal difference (TD) updates:
\begin{equation}
Q(s_t, a_t) \leftarrow Q(s_t, a_t) + \alpha\!\left[r_t + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s_{t+1}, a') - Q(s_t, a_t)\right],
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is the learning rate. The bracketed quantity is the TD error $\delta_t$.

\subsection{Deep Q-Networks}

When the state space is large, tabular Q-learning is infeasible. Deep Q-Networks (DQN) approximate $Q^*$ with a neural network $Q(s, a; \theta)$ parameterized by weights $\theta$ \citep{Mnih2013, Mnih2015}.

Two mechanisms stabilize training. First, \emph{experience replay} stores transitions $(s, a, r, s')$ in a buffer and samples mini-batches uniformly, breaking temporal correlations. Second, a \emph{target network} with parameters $\theta^-$ provides stable TD targets. The loss for a sampled transition is:
\begin{equation}
L(\theta) = \left[r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s', a'; \theta^-) - Q(s, a; \theta)\right]^2.
\end{equation}
The target parameters $\theta^-$ are periodically copied from $\theta$.

Exploration uses an $\varepsilon$-greedy policy: with probability $\varepsilon$ a random action is taken, otherwise the greedy action $\arg\max_a Q(s, a; \theta)$.

\subsection{DQN Extensions}
\label{sec:dqn_extensions}

Several extensions address specific weaknesses of the basic algorithm.

\paragraph{Double DQN.} Standard DQN overestimates action values because the same network both selects and evaluates the maximizing action. Double DQN \citep{vanHasselt2016} decouples these steps:
\begin{equation}
y = r + \gamma\, Q\!\left(s',\; \arg\max_{a'} Q(s', a'; \theta);\; \theta^-\right).
\end{equation}
The online network selects the action; the target network evaluates it.

\paragraph{Dueling architecture.} The Dueling network \citep{Wang2016} decomposes the Q-function into a state value $V(s)$ and an action advantage $A(s, a)$:
\begin{equation}
Q(s, a) = V(s) + A(s, a) - \frac{1}{|A|}\sum_{a'} A(s, a'),
\end{equation}
where $|A|$ is the number of actions. This decomposition allows the network to learn state values without evaluating every action, which helps in states where the action choice matters little.

\paragraph{Prioritized experience replay.} Uniform replay treats all transitions equally. Prioritized experience replay (PER) \citep{Schaul2016} samples transitions proportionally to their TD error magnitude, revisiting surprising transitions more often. The sampling probability of transition $i$ is:
\begin{equation}
P(i) = \frac{p_i^\alpha}{\sum_k p_k^\alpha}, \quad p_i = |\delta_i| + \epsilon_p,
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ controls prioritization strength and $\epsilon_p$ is a small constant ensuring nonzero probability. Non-uniform sampling introduces bias, corrected by importance-sampling (IS) weights:
\begin{equation}
w_i = \left(\frac{1}{N \cdot P(i)}\right)^\beta\!\bigg/\; \max_j w_j,
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the number of stored transitions and $\beta$ is annealed from a starting value to 1 during training, gradually applying the full bias correction.

\paragraph{Multi-step returns.} Instead of bootstrapping from the immediate next state, $n$-step returns \citep{Sutton1988} accumulate rewards over $n$ steps:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:nstep}
R_t^{(n)} = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \gamma^k r_{t+k+1} + \gamma^n \max_{a'} Q(s_{t+n}, a'; \theta^-).
\end{equation}
This propagates reward information faster at the cost of increased variance. Moderate values of $n$ (typically 2--5) improve learning speed in practice.

\paragraph{Rainbow.} \citet{Hessel2018} combined six DQN extensions into a single agent: Double DQN, Dueling, PER, multi-step returns, distributional RL (C51), and NoisyNet. An ablation study showed that each component contributes, with prioritized replay and multi-step returns providing the largest individual gains.

We adopt four of these six components: Double DQN, Dueling, PER, and 3-step returns. We omit distributional RL due to implementation complexity and NoisyNet because $\varepsilon$-greedy exploration is sufficient at smaller scales \citep{ObandoCeron2021, Fortunato2018}.

\subsection{Training Stability Techniques}
\label{sec:stability}

\paragraph{Polyak soft target updates.} The original DQN copies $\theta$ to $\theta^-$ every $C$ steps, creating discontinuities in the targets. Polyak averaging \citep{Polyak1992, Lillicrap2016} replaces the hard copy with an exponential moving average, updating the target at every step:
\begin{equation}
\theta^- \leftarrow (1 - \tau)\,\theta^- + \tau\,\theta,
\end{equation}
with a small $\tau$. This produces smoother target evolution and removes the need to tune $C$.

\paragraph{AdamW.} The Adam optimizer applies L2 regularization to the gradient, which interacts poorly with adaptive learning rates. AdamW \citep{Loshchilov2019} decouples weight decay from the gradient update, providing effective regularization that helps maintain network plasticity during long training runs.

\paragraph{LayerNorm.} Batch normalization is unreliable in RL because the data distribution is non-stationary and effective batch sizes are small. LayerNorm \citep{Ba2016} normalizes across features within each sample, independent of the batch, making it suitable for the single-sample updates common in RL.

\paragraph{Learning rate scheduling.} The 1cycle policy \citep{Smith2019} ramps the learning rate from a low value to a peak over a short warmup phase, then cosine-anneals it back down. This provides implicit regularization and can accelerate convergence compared to a fixed learning rate.

\paragraph{Plasticity.} Neural networks in RL can lose the ability to learn over extended training \citep{Lyle2023, Dohare2024}. Weight decay and normalization are effective countermeasures \citep{Bjorck2021}. Our combination of AdamW, LayerNorm, and learning rate annealing addresses this concern.

\subsection{Reward Shaping}
\label{sec:reward_shaping_bg}

When multiple reward components are combined with different weights, the agent maximizes the weighted sum, which may not correspond to any single intended behavior. This kind of mismatch is commonly described as reward hacking \citep{Amodei2016}. In a survival setting, it creates a tension: a high food reward produces aggressive foraging at the cost of safety, a high death penalty produces shelter-seeking at the cost of starvation, and a high shelter reward produces passive camping. The interaction between these components, not just their individual magnitudes, determines the learned policy.

This interaction is the central object of study. We fix the agent and vary only the reward weights to isolate their effect on behavior.

\section{Environment}
\label{sec:environment}
% =============================================================================

Grid-based environments are a common testbed for reinforcement learning, from safety-focused gridworlds \citep{Leike2017} to the modular Minigrid framework \citep{ChevalierBoisvert2023}. Our environment extends this tradition with a multi-objective survival task that foregrounds the reward design problem.

\subsection{World Design}
\label{sec:world_design}

The environment is a $64 \times 64$ bounded grid with discrete time steps. An episode ends when the agent dies (health reaches zero) or when a safety cap is reached. We use a cap of $50{,}000$ ticks for all reported experiments: large enough that no trained policy approaches it, and sufficient to catch pathological infinite-loop policies without truncating normal play. The world contains four entity types.

\paragraph{Agent.} A single agent with two resource bars: health $h$ and hunger $u$, both with maximum value $h_{\max}=u_{\max}=100$. The agent spawns at the grid center with both bars full. Hunger depletes by 0.2 per tick and by an additional 0.5 when moving. When hunger reaches zero, the agent starves and loses 0.5 health per tick. When hunger exceeds 30\% of maximum, health regenerates at 0.5 per tick up to the cap.

\paragraph{Enemies.} Mobile entities that deal 15 damage on contact (Manhattan distance $\leq 1$). Each tick, an enemy moves with probability 0.5. Within a vision range of 3 (Manhattan distance), enemies pursue the agent along the axis of greatest distance. Outside this range, they move randomly. Enemies cannot enter shelter tiles or tiles occupied by another enemy. The world supports up to 8 enemies simultaneously, with roughly a third of this capacity present at episode start. New enemies appear with probability 0.01 per tick.

\paragraph{Food.} Static items that restore 20 hunger when consumed by stepping onto their tile. Up to 36 food items exist simultaneously, with half present at episode start. New food appears with probability 0.05 per tick.

\paragraph{Shelters.} Static tiles generated at episode start with density 0.02 per cell (approximately 82 shelters per episode). While in a shelter, the agent takes no damage from enemies. Shelters do not protect against starvation.

Each episode uses a different random seed, producing a unique shelter layout and initial entity placement. Enemy and food spawning continues throughout the episode, maintaining population pressure.

\subsection{Observation Space}

The agent observes through an egocentric $15 \times 15$ window (radius 7). The observation consists of three binary spatial channels:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item Enemy presence
\item Food presence
\item Shelter presence
\end{enumerate}
Cells outside the grid boundary are zero. Three scalar features are appended: $\frac{h}{h_{\max}}$, $\frac{u}{u_{\max}}$, and a binary shelter indicator. The full observation is a 678-dimensional vector (675 spatial + 3 scalar). Figure~\ref{fig:obs_space} illustrates the layout.

\subsection{Action Space}

The agent has five discrete actions: stay in place, or move one cell up, down, left, or right. Movement is clamped to the grid boundaries.

\subsection{Reward Structure}
\label{sec:reward_structure}

The reward at each step is a weighted sum of up to eleven components:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:reward}
\begin{aligned}
r_t ={}& w_\text{food}\, i_t^{(\text{eat})}
    + w_\text{edm}\, d_t^{(\text{e})}
    + w_\text{sdm}\, d_t^{(\text{s})}
    + w_\text{death}\, i_t^{(\text{die})} \\
& + w_\text{hunger}\, i_t^{(\text{low})}
    + w_\text{hprop}(1 - u_t)
    + w_\text{tick} \\
& + w_\text{fprox}\, \Delta\phi_t^{(\text{f})}
    + w_\text{eprox}\, \Delta\phi_t^{(\text{e})}
    + w_\text{sprox}\, \Delta\phi_t^{(\text{s})} \\
& + w_\text{shelter}\, i_t^{(\text{safe})},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $w_{\cdot} \in \mathbb{R}$ are tunable scalar weights; $i_t^{(\text{eat})}, i_t^{(\text{die})}, i_t^{(\text{low})}, i_t^{(\text{safe})} \in \{0,1\}$ are binary indicators for food eaten, death, low hunger, and shelter safety; $d_t^{(\text{e})}$ and $d_t^{(\text{s})}$ are enemy damage and starvation damage; and $u_t \in [0, 1]$ is the hunger ratio.

The three proximity terms use potential-based reward shaping (PBRS) in delta form. For target type $x \in \{\text{f}, \text{e}, \text{s}\}$ (food, enemy, shelter), define the closeness potential
\begin{equation}
\phi_t^{(x)} = \frac{d_\text{max} - d_t^{(x)} + 1}{d_\text{max} + 1} \in [0, 1],
\end{equation}
where $d_t^{(x)}$ is the Manhattan distance to the nearest visible entity of type $x$ and $d_\text{max} = 2r_\text{obs}$. If no entity is visible, $\phi_t^{(x)} = 0$. The delta reward $\Delta\phi_t^{(x)} = \phi_t^{(x)} - \phi_{t-1}^{(x)}$ is positive when the agent moves closer and negative when it moves away, satisfying the PBRS form of \citet{Ng1999}. Unlike absolute proximity, delta proximity does not reward hovering near a target.

Two proximity terms can be conditionally gated on the hunger ratio. When conditional gating is enabled, the food proximity fires only when $u_t < u_\text{thresh}$ (hungry) and the shelter proximity fires only when $u_t \geq u_\text{thresh}$ (well-fed). This encodes a behavioral mode switch through the reward function: the agent receives a spatial gradient toward food when it needs to eat and toward shelter when it does not.

These components create competing pressures. Collecting food requires movement, which costs hunger and exposes the agent to enemies. Shelters neutralize enemy damage but provide no food. The hunger-proportional penalty grows every tick, creating urgency to eat that the agent cannot escape through passivity.

A subtle design consideration is the relative magnitude of continuous per-tick rewards versus sparse event rewards. A per-tick reward of $+0.1$ accumulated over a 700-step episode produces $+70$, which can dominate a food reward of $+5.0$. This creates incentives for passive survival: the agent learns to avoid risk rather than seek food. Similarly, a food proximity reward that is too large relative to the food-eaten reward incentivizes hovering near food without eating it, since eating removes the food and terminates the proximity stream. These failure modes motivate the experimental configurations in Section~\ref{sec:experiments}.

These weights are the primary independent variable in our experiments.

\section{Agent Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Network}
\label{sec:network}

Since this work isolates the effect of reward shaping, the architecture must be adequate for the task but does not need to be optimal. The key requirement is sufficient representational capacity so that the architecture is not the bottleneck. A convolutional encoder is the natural choice because the observation has spatial structure ($3 \times 15 \times 15$ grid). The Dueling head is inherited from the Rainbow-lite recipe. Channel counts and hidden dimensions follow standard values from the literature rather than task-specific tuning. The resulting network has 970{,}438 trainable parameters for a 678-dimensional input with 5 output actions, providing ample capacity for the relatively simple decision problem. Specific architectural choices (depth, width, kernel sizes) are not expected to materially affect the reward shaping analysis.

The encoder splits the input into a $3 \times 15 \times 15$ spatial grid and 3 scalar features. Three convolutional layers process the spatial grid:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item $\text{Conv2d}(3 \to 32,\; 3 \times 3,\; \text{stride}\ 1,\; \text{pad}\ 1)$ \textrightarrow\ ReLU \textrightarrow\ LayerNorm
\item $\text{Conv2d}(32 \to 64,\; 3 \times 3,\; \text{stride}\ 1,\; \text{pad}\ 1)$ \textrightarrow\ ReLU \textrightarrow\ LayerNorm
\item $\text{Conv2d}(64 \to 64,\; 3 \times 3,\; \text{stride}\ 2,\; \text{pad}\ 0)$ \textrightarrow\ ReLU
\end{enumerate}
The third layer reduces the spatial dimensions from $15 \times 15$ to $7 \times 7$, producing a 3136-dimensional feature vector. This is concatenated with the 3 scalar features and projected through a fully connected layer ($3139 \to 256$) with ReLU and LayerNorm.

The Dueling head splits the 256-dimensional representation into two streams. The value stream maps $256 \to 128 \to 1$. The advantage stream maps $256 \to 128 \to 5$. Q-values are computed as $Q(s,a) = V(s) + A(s,a) - \bar{A}(s)$.

Convolutional layers use Kaiming uniform initialization. Fully connected layers use Xavier uniform initialization. The output layers of both Dueling streams use orthogonal initialization with gain 0.01, producing near-zero initial Q-values and avoiding large initial TD errors.

\subsection{Training Algorithm}
\label{sec:training_algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:training} summarizes the training procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Rainbow-lite DQN training.}
\label{alg:training}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Initialize policy network $\theta$, target network $\theta^- \leftarrow \theta$
\State Initialize prioritized replay buffer $\mathcal{D}$, $n$-step accumulator
\State Collect 10{,}000 transitions with random policy into $\mathcal{D}$
\For{$t = 1, \ldots, T$}
    \State Select $a_t$ via $\varepsilon$-greedy from $Q(\cdot; \theta)$, decay $\varepsilon$
    \State Execute $a_t$, observe $r_t, s_{t+1}, d_t$
    \State Compute $n$-step return; commit to $\mathcal{D}$ when ready
    \State Sample batch from $\mathcal{D}$ with PER; compute importance-sampling (IS) weights $w_i$
    \State $y_i = R_i^{(n)} + (1 - d_i)\,\gamma^n\, Q\!\left(s_i',\, \arg\max_{a'} Q(s_i', a'; \theta);\, \theta^-\right)$
    \State $L = \frac{1}{B}\sum_i w_i \cdot \text{Huber}(Q(s_i, a_i; \theta),\; y_i)$
    \State Update $\theta$ via AdamW with gradient clipping; step LR scheduler
    \State $\theta^- \leftarrow (1-\tau)\,\theta^- + \tau\,\theta$
    \State Update priorities in $\mathcal{D}$ from new TD errors
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
Here, $d_i \in \{0,1\}$ indicates whether sampled transition $i$ is terminal, and $B$ denotes the batch size.
\paragraph{Experience collection.} At each step, the transition $(s_t, a_t, r_t, s_{t+1}, d_t)$ enters an $n$-step accumulator. Once $n = 3$ transitions have accumulated, the oldest is committed to the replay buffer with return $R_t^{(3)} = r_t + \gamma r_{t+1} + \gamma^2 r_{t+2}$, bootstrap state $s_{t+3}$, and discount factor $\gamma^3$. At episode termination (agent death), remaining entries are flushed with shortened returns. On episode truncation (time limit), pending entries are discarded to prevent cross-episode contamination of $n$-step returns.

The distinction between termination and truncation matters for the bootstrap target. A terminated episode (death) sets $d = 1$, zeroing the bootstrap term. A truncated episode sets $d = 0$, allowing bootstrapping from the final state since the agent was still alive.

\paragraph{Replay buffer.} Transitions are stored in pre-allocated numpy arrays. A SumTree data structure supports $O(\log n)$ priority updates and proportional sampling. Stratified sampling divides $[0, P_\text{total}]$ into equal segments and draws one sample per segment, reducing variance compared to pure proportional sampling.

\paragraph{Optimization.} AdamW with decoupled weight decay ($10^{-4}$) on convolutional and linear weights; bias and normalization parameters are exempt. Gradients are clipped to norm 1.0. The learning rate follows a 1cycle schedule: 5\% warmup from $10^{-5}$ to $10^{-4}$, then cosine annealing to $10^{-6}$.

\paragraph{Exploration.} $\varepsilon$-greedy with linear decay from 1.0 to 0.05 over the first half of training. The relatively high final $\varepsilon$ ensures continued exploration in the stochastic environment.

\subsection{Hyperparameters}
\label{sec:hyperparameters}

Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams} lists all hyperparameters. Values follow established defaults from the literature rather than environment-specific tuning. The focus of this work is reward shaping, so all hyperparameters are held constant across experiments.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

Each configuration trains for $T = 2{,}000{,}000$ timesteps (roughly 8 hours on consumer hardware). Greedy evaluation (20 episodes, $\varepsilon = 0$) runs every 5{,}000 steps during training. Final benchmarks evaluate the best checkpoint over 100 episodes with fixed seeds (1000--1099). We report four behavioral metrics: mean survival time (ticks), mean food eaten, mean damage taken, and death rate. Each configuration runs with 3 random seeds for variance estimation. The agent architecture and all hyperparameters (Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams}) are held constant across reward shaping experiments.

\paragraph{Heuristic baseline.} A scripted agent provides a reference for non-learned behavior. The heuristic camps in the nearest shelter and leaves only when the hunger ratio drops below 0.5, at which point it moves toward the nearest food. It flees from enemies within Manhattan distance 5. Over 100 evaluation episodes, this agent survives 768 steps on average, eats 1.19 food items per episode, and dies in 95\% of episodes. The remaining 5\% reach the 1000-step time limit.

\subsection{Failure Modes}
\label{sec:failure_modes}

Early experiments revealed three degenerate policies that emerged reliably under certain reward configurations. These modes are not mutually exclusive: all three appeared within a single baseline training run at different stages and in different episodes, with the agent alternating between hovering near food, seeking enemies, and oscillating in place depending on the local state distribution.

\paragraph{Oscillation.} The agent remains passive or oscillates between adjacent cells. This behavior was observed under several reward configurations and its precise cause requires further investigation. One hypothesis is that a per-tick survival reward can make passivity rational: with $w_\text{tick} = 0.1$ and $\gamma = 0.99$, a stationary agent surviving 700 ticks earns $\approx +10.0$ from tick rewards while the death penalty discounted to $t = 700$ contributes only $-10 \times 0.99^{700} \approx -0.009$. However, oscillation also appeared in configurations without a tick reward, suggesting additional contributing factors such as training dynamics or exploration failures.

\paragraph{Food hovering.} The agent moves near food but does not eat it. This occurs when the food proximity reward exceeds the hunger penalty. An agent 5 cells from food with $w_\text{prox} = 0.5$ earns $+0.33$ per tick from proximity, while the average hunger penalty is $-0.15$ per tick, yielding a positive net reward for inaction. Eating the food would remove the proximity stream. Heuristic H2 addresses this by bounding $w_\text{prox}$ below $|w_\text{hprop}|/2$.

\paragraph{Suicide.} When the proximity gradient is too weak to guide the agent toward food, the agent cannot learn to forage. Without foraging, every tick accumulates hunger-proportional penalty. A stationary agent surviving 700 ticks until starvation accumulates approximately $-135$ from $w_\text{hprop}$ and $-100$ from $w_\text{sdm}$, totaling $-235$ before the death penalty. The agent discovers that dying quickly is preferable: seeking enemies yields a fast death at total cost $\approx -24$ (a few enemy hits plus the death penalty), roughly 10$\times$ less than slow starvation. This is a rational response to a reward function where living is more expensive than dying.

The food hovering and suicide failure modes are clear instances of reward hacking~\citep{Amodei2016}: the agent optimizes the reward function as specified, but the resulting behavior diverges from the designer's intent. The cause of oscillation is less certain and requires further investigation. These failure modes motivate the design heuristics derived in Section~\ref{sec:reward_configs}.

\subsection{Reward Configurations}
\label{sec:reward_configs}

The failure modes above show that poorly chosen reward weights produce degenerate policies. Based on the per-step reward dynamics, we propose four design heuristics (H1--H4) that help avoid the food hovering and suicide failure modes. These compare quantities at the same timestep, so discounting does not affect them.

Let $D = 2 r_\text{obs}$ denote the observation diameter, $c$ the per-step movement hunger cost, $d_e$ the enemy contact damage, and $h_s$ the per-tick starvation damage.
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{H\arabic*},nosep,leftmargin=2.5em]
  \item \emph{Anti-stasis.} One step toward food must yield more proximity reward than the hunger penalty from the movement cost:
    $w_\text{prox} > |w_\text{hprop}| \cdot c \cdot (D+1) \,/\, u_\text{max}$.
  \item \emph{Anti-hovering.} Maximum proximity reward must not exceed the average hunger penalty:
    $w_\text{prox} < |w_\text{hprop}| / 2$.
  \item \emph{Enemy flee.} Damage penalty from one hit must exceed the hunger penalty from one evasive step:
    $|w_\text{edm}| \cdot d_e > |w_\text{hprop}| \cdot c \,/\, u_\text{max}$.
  \item \emph{Starvation escalation.} Per-tick penalty when starving must exceed the max hunger-proportional penalty:
    $|w_\text{sdm}| \cdot h_s > |w_\text{hprop}|$.
\end{enumerate}

The food hovering failure mode maps to an H2 violation and the suicide failure mode to an H1 violation.

Table~\ref{tab:reward_configs} defines the diagnostic configurations. The \textsc{baseline} satisfies all four heuristics. The three diagnostic configurations each test a specific aspect of the reward function.

\paragraph{Baseline.} The default weights (Table~\ref{tab:default_rewards}) satisfy all four heuristics. Zero survival tick eliminates passive reward, strong hunger-proportional penalty ($-0.3$) creates urgency, and the food proximity gradient ($+0.1$) provides a directional signal without dominating the reward budget. This is the primary configuration expected to produce active foraging.

\paragraph{Weak proximity.} Reduces $w_\text{prox}$ from $0.1$ to $0.02$, violating H1: the proximity gain from one step toward food ($0.02$) falls below the hunger penalty from the movement cost ($|w_\text{hprop}| \cdot c \cdot (D+1) / u_\text{max} = 0.0225$). Without a viable foraging strategy, the agent converges to the suicide failure mode: seeking enemies to die quickly or oscillating to accelerate starvation, both of which truncate the accumulating hunger penalty.

\paragraph{No death.} Removes the death penalty ($w_\text{death} = 0$). All per-step shaping signals remain. Tests whether a terminal signal is necessary for learning survival, or whether the accumulated per-step penalties (hunger, starvation damage, enemy damage) are sufficient on their own.

\paragraph{Death only.} Removes all per-step rewards, leaving only the terminal death penalty ($w_\text{death} = -10$). The agent receives zero signal until the episode ends in death. This is the sparsest possible reward for the survival task and tests the credit assignment limit: can the agent attribute a death at $t = 700$ to actions taken hundreds of steps earlier, with no intermediate gradient?

\subsection{Reward Engineering}
\label{sec:engineered}

The diagnostic configurations confirm that heuristic violations produce predictable failures. The next question is whether a carefully designed reward function can match the heuristic. The design process identified two key insights: a structural incompatibility in the baseline, and the counterproductive effect of reward complexity.

\paragraph{Structural incompatibility of the hunger-proportional penalty.}
The baseline reward includes a continuous hunger-proportional penalty $w_\text{hprop}(1-u_t) = -0.3(1-u_t)$. Every movement costs $c = 0.5$ hunger, triggering an incremental penalty of $|w_\text{hprop}| \cdot c / u_\text{max} = 0.0015$ per step. At $\gamma = 0.99$, the present value of this penalty stream over the expected remaining episode is
\begin{equation}
\text{PV}_\text{move} = \frac{0.0015}{1 - 0.99} = 0.15,
\end{equation}
while the maximum food proximity reward from one step is approximately $0.1 / 15 \approx 0.0067$. The movement penalty exceeds the approach reward by a factor of 22. Not moving is Q-value optimal regardless of hunger level. No proximity gradient can overcome this stasis equilibrium. Removing $w_\text{hprop}$ is a prerequisite for any proximity-based design.

\paragraph{Conditional proximity gating.}
With the hunger-proportional penalty removed, the reward function can encode the three behavioral modes (forage, shelter, flee) through conditional PBRS delta proximity signals. The food proximity gradient ($w_\text{fprox} = 0.15$) activates only when the hunger ratio falls below 0.5. The shelter proximity gradient ($w_\text{sprox} = 0.15$) activates only above this threshold. The enemy proximity gradient ($w_\text{eprox} = -0.5$) is always active. This gating mirrors the heuristic's conditional logic through the reward function: the agent receives a spatial gradient toward food when it needs to eat and toward shelter when it does not.

\paragraph{The minimal reward set.}
We tested configurations with varying numbers of reward components. Configurations combining proximity gradients with event-driven rewards (food eaten, damage taken), threshold penalties (low hunger), and binary signals (shelter safety, survival tick) consistently underperformed a minimal configuration using only the three conditional proximity gradients and the death penalty. All other weights are set to zero. Table~\ref{tab:minimal_config} lists the complete weight vector.

This configuration removes all event-driven rewards, threshold penalties, and binary signals. The hypothesis is that fewer signals reduce interference in Q-value estimation. The three proximity gradients encode the behavioral mode switch directly, and the death penalty provides a terminal signal for TD bootstrapping.

Note that this minimal configuration renders all four heuristics (H1--H4) vacuous, since they involve $w_\text{hprop}$ which is zero. The heuristics were useful as analytical tools that guided the design away from degenerate weight combinations, not as active bounds on the final configuration.

\paragraph{The foraging gap.}
E5 learns coherent shelter and flee behavior but barely forages: 0.66 food per episode versus the heuristic's 1.19. The survival gap of 54 ticks corresponds to the food deficit: roughly 0.6 fewer food items at approximately 100 ticks of additional life per item.

We tested explicit food rewards ($w_\text{food}$) at magnitudes from 0.3 to 5.0. At 5.0, the food reward causes the agent to pursue food through enemy zones; survival drops to 470. At lower magnitudes, the agent forages more but takes more damage; survival does not exceed E5's shelter-camping policy. No tested magnitude produces both increased foraging and maintained enemy avoidance.

\subsection{Bottleneck 2: Temporal Context}
\label{sec:temporal}

The reward search saturated below the heuristic, so the question becomes what else limits the agent. A plausible candidate is the observation: the single-frame $15 \times 15$ grid shows positions but not motion. The agent cannot distinguish an approaching enemy from a retreating one, nor its own last movement direction. Because our proximity rewards are defined as potential deltas (Section~\ref{sec:reward_structure}), the optimal policy inherently depends on quantities the agent cannot observe directly.

Frame stacking \citep{Mnih2015} provides temporal context by concatenating the spatial grids from the last $k$ time steps. The base observation has 3 spatial channels on a $15 \times 15$ grid; a 4-frame stack provides 12 channels. An enemy occupying different cells across consecutive frames reveals its direction and speed. The scalar features (health, hunger, shelter indicator) are taken from the current step only, since they already reflect the agent's cumulative state.

\paragraph{Effect on the state representation.} The single-frame observation space has dimension $|O_1| = 3 \cdot 15 \cdot 15 + 3 = 678$. With $k$-frame stacking the spatial portion grows by a factor of $k$ while the scalars are unchanged: $|O_k| = 3k \cdot 15 \cdot 15 + 3$. For $k = 4$ this gives $|O_4| = 2703$, a factor of 3.99 increase in observation dimension. The number of distinguishable histories the agent can condition its policy on grows roughly as $|S|^k$ in principle, since a sequence of $k$ frames distinguishes trajectories that collide in the single-frame representation. In practice the growth is smaller because most 4-tuples of consecutive grids are not reachable under the environment dynamics, but the agent gains access to derived quantities (velocity, approach direction) that were previously invisible.

\paragraph{Result.} We train E5 with $k = 4$ frames for 5{,}000{,}000 steps. The replay buffer is reduced from 1{,}000{,}000 to 250{,}000 transitions to accommodate the larger observation size. All other hyperparameters follow Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams} except for slower target updates ($\tau = 0.002$). The best checkpoint (seed 44) benchmarks at $811 \pm 25$ survival with $4.12$ food per episode and $12\%$ time-limit rate ($p = 0.007$), the first DQN configuration to significantly outperform the heuristic. The reward was already sufficient; the perceptual bottleneck was the binding constraint.

\paragraph{But the policy collapses.} The frame-stacked agent reaches its peak early, around 350K steps, and then regresses as training continues (Figure~\ref{fig:collapse}). The best-checkpoint result is real, but the policy is not sustained. This sets up the third bottleneck.

\subsection{Bottleneck 3: Training Dynamics}
\label{sec:plasticity}

The collapse pattern in Figure~\ref{fig:collapse} is consistent with recent work on \emph{primacy bias} \citep{Nikishin2022} and \emph{plasticity loss} \citep{Dohare2024, Abbas2023}: as training progresses, the network loses the ability to incorporate new evidence. In our setup the mechanism is self-reinforcing. Once epsilon decays and the policy becomes deterministic, the replay buffer fills with transitions from the current (already committed) policy. Foraging transitions age out; the Q-network trains on biased data; the policy drifts further toward whatever behavior dominates the buffer. The decaying learning rate of the OneCycle schedule compounds this: by the time the buffer shifts, the effective LR is too small to correct.

We test three interventions, each attacking the failure mode from a different angle. All share the same base: E5 rewards plus $w_\text{food} = 0.3$, 4-frame stacking, $2{,}000{,}000$ steps, $\tau = 0.002$, buffer $250{,}000$.

\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=1.6em]
  \item \textbf{Constant learning rate (V7$_\mathrm{fs}$)}. Replace the OneCycle schedule with a constant $10^{-4}$. Preserves the network's capacity to update throughout training.
  \item \textbf{Cyclical exploration (V8$_\mathrm{fs,cycle}$)}. In addition to constant LR, reset $\varepsilon$ to $0.5$ every $500{,}000$ steps. Directly refills the replay buffer with diverse transitions on a fixed cadence.
  \item \textbf{Periodic head resets (V8$_\mathrm{fs,reset}$)}. In addition to constant LR, reset the Dueling head weights to a fresh initialization every $500{,}000$ steps while keeping the CNN encoder and replay buffer. This is the reset recipe from \citet{Nikishin2022}, applied to only the last layers.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/plasticity_interventions}
\caption{Training-dynamics interventions compared. All three share the same reward and frame-stacking setup; they differ only in how training dynamics are managed. Dark line is the best seed, light lines are the other two seeds. Dots mark peak eval. Head resets (V8$_\mathrm{fs,reset}$) produce the highest and most durable trajectories.}
\label{fig:plasticity}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Constant learning rate alone.} Replacing the OneCycle schedule with constant $10^{-4}$ (V7$_\mathrm{fs}$) produces the most consistent result across seeds. All three seeds cluster within $7$ ticks of each other ($829.6$, $834.7$, $836.2$) and all significantly beat the heuristic ($p \leq 0.03$). The per-seed $95\%$ CI widths are comparable to the heuristic's ($\pm 34$ ticks vs heuristic $\pm 20$). This is the lowest-variance improvement in the study: removing the LR decay alone is sufficient to produce a reliable agent.

\paragraph{Layered interventions (V8$_\mathrm{fs,cycle}$, V8$_\mathrm{fs,reset}$).} Adding cyclical epsilon ($\varepsilon$ reset to $0.5$ every $500{,}000$ steps) or periodic head resets (Dueling head re-initialized every $500{,}000$ steps) produces higher single-seed peaks but with substantially higher seed-to-seed variance. V8$_\mathrm{fs,cycle}$ has two seeds at $866$ and $877$ survival (both $p < 0.001$) and one seed at $637$ that significantly loses to the heuristic ($p < 0.001$). V8$_\mathrm{fs,reset}$ has one strong seed at $905$ ($p < 0.001$), one seed at $743$ tied with the heuristic, and one seed at $717$ that loses to the heuristic. Each of these interventions injects periodic perturbations into training; the resulting data distribution is more diverse but also more sensitive to seed randomness.

\paragraph{Combining resets with stronger signals (V9$_\mathrm{fs,strong\_reset}$).}
Doubling the food reward and food proximity weight ($w_\text{food} = 0.5$, $w_\text{fprox} = 0.3$) by itself destabilizes the flee behavior and underperforms the heuristic in 2 of 3 seeds (Table~\ref{tab:results}, ``stronger signals alone'' row). Adding periodic head resets to the same stronger reward configuration rescues this regime. The best seed (42) reaches mean survival $943.7$ with a median episode of $924$ ticks and per-episode food consumption of $7.82$; across all three seeds the longest single observed episode reaches $2{,}477$ ticks (seed 43). The two weaker seeds of V9$_\mathrm{fs,strong\_reset}$ land at $854$ and $731$ survival, so the best-seed $944$ should be read as the top end of a wide distribution rather than a reliable operating point. This pattern --- best-seed peak scaling with intervention complexity, variance scaling with it too --- holds across all layered configurations.

\paragraph{A finite ceiling.}
Every seed of every configuration in the study has a $100\%$ death rate; no episode approaches the $50{,}000$-tick safety cap. The best policy extends single-episode survival up to $2{,}477$ ticks but does not approach unbounded survival within the tested training budget.

\subsection{Summary of Observations}
\label{sec:analysis}

Five empirical patterns emerge from the experiments.

\paragraph{Fewer reward components produce better policies.}
E5 with four reward components outperforms E4 with nine (706 vs 695 mean survival), despite receiving less information per step.

\paragraph{Reward scaling does not resolve the foraging gap.}
Adding a food reward to E5 at magnitudes from $0.3$ to $5.0$ either degrades enemy avoidance (high values) or fails to improve survival (low values). Extended training ($5$M steps) also does not help: all seeds peak early and decline.

\paragraph{Temporal context is a hard constraint for delta-based rewards.}
PBRS delta proximity rewards depend on position changes, but the single-frame observation cannot distinguish motion from stasis. Frame stacking provides the missing information and lifts performance above the heuristic without changing the reward at all.

\paragraph{Policy collapse is a training-dynamics problem, not a reward problem.}
The collapse occurs with the same reward function that produced the peak. Fixing it requires either preserving plasticity (constant LR), actively refreshing it (head resets), or preventing the buffer bias that drives it (cyclical $\varepsilon$).

\paragraph{Constant LR gives the most reliable improvement.}
Replacing the OneCycle LR schedule with a constant rate (V7$_\mathrm{fs}$) produces the lowest-variance result in the study: all three seeds land between $829.6$ and $836.2$ mean survival, all significantly beating the heuristic. No other FS configuration has this level of seed-to-seed consistency.

\paragraph{Layered interventions trade consistency for peak.}
Periodic head resets (V8$_\mathrm{fs,reset}$), cyclical $\varepsilon$ (V8$_\mathrm{fs,cycle}$), and stronger foraging signals combined with resets (V9$_\mathrm{fs,strong\_reset}$) each push the best-seed peak higher ($905$, $877$, $944$ respectively) but each introduces at least one seed that regresses below the heuristic. Expressed as mean across seeds, these layered configurations sit at or below V7$_\mathrm{fs}$'s $834$: $794$ for V8$_\mathrm{fs,cycle}$, $788$ for V8$_\mathrm{fs,reset}$, $843$ for V9$_\mathrm{fs,strong\_reset}$.

\paragraph{Head resets rescue otherwise-destabilizing reward signals.}
Doubling the food and food-proximity weights alone ($w_\text{food}=0.5$, $w_\text{fprox}=0.3$) underperforms the heuristic in 2 of 3 seeds. Adding head resets to the same stronger reward configuration yields the highest single-seed result in the study ($944$) with per-episode food consumption of $7.82$ (roughly $6.5\times$ the heuristic's $1.19$). The reset mechanism is not merely a plasticity patch; it widens the range of reward functions under which Rainbow-lite DQN can train without collapsing. However, the rescue does not produce a consistent policy: the other two seeds of V9 land at $854$ and $731$.

\paragraph{Frame stacking enables foraging.}
The only modification that produces foraging behavior competitive with the heuristic is frame stacking. The reward function is unchanged; only the observation changes. The frame-stacking agent eats 4.12 food per episode (vs 0.66 without), but the foraging policy is transient: survival peaks early in training (856--867 at step 350K) and declines as training continues. The best checkpoint captures this transient behavior.

\paragraph{The agent extends survival but is not unbounded.}
With the $50{,}000$-tick safety cap, every seed of the best configuration has a $100\%$ death rate; no episode approaches the cap; the longest single episode observed across all three seeds is $2477$ ticks (seed 43). The best-seed agent's median episode is $924$ ticks (seed 42), roughly $22\%$ longer than the heuristic's $754$. The agent does not achieve indefinite survival within the tested training budget.

\paragraph{Delta proximity does not cause hovering.}
Absolute proximity would reward the agent for being near food, creating the hovering failure mode (Section~\ref{sec:failure_modes}). Delta proximity rewards only the change in distance. All engineered configurations use delta proximity, and none exhibit hovering.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% =============================================================================

\paragraph{Reward design as constrained optimization.}
The design heuristics H1--H4 formalize per-step reward dynamics as inequalities over the weight vector. Satisfying all heuristics is necessary but not sufficient: the baseline satisfies all four yet fails due to the structural incompatibility between hunger-proportional penalty and movement cost. The progression from E4 to E5 shows that removing harmful signals can be more effective than adding informative ones. The heuristics rule out two specific degenerate policies (hovering, suicide) but do not guarantee the intended behavior.

\paragraph{The perceptual bottleneck.}
The reward-shaping search converged on a configuration that was coherent, PBRS-compliant, and analytically defensible --- yet still produced an agent that consistently under-forages. Frame stacking, a single well-known observation-level change \citep{Mnih2015}, unlocked the policy the reward had been aiming for. This is consistent with the recent framing that optimization rather than exploration is often the binding constraint in DeepRL \citep{Tarbouriech2025}: the reward signal was adequate, but the agent could not exploit it without temporal context. A reviewer or practitioner encountering a saturating reward-shaping campaign should check the observation before concluding that the reward is misspecified.

\paragraph{The training-dynamics bottleneck.}
The collapse pattern in Figure~\ref{fig:collapse} matches descriptions of \emph{primacy bias} \citep{Nikishin2022} and \emph{plasticity loss} \citep{Dohare2024, Abbas2023}: as training progresses, the network loses the ability to incorporate new evidence, even when that evidence is present in the buffer. In our setup this manifests as a self-reinforcing loop between the $\varepsilon$-greedy exploration schedule and the OneCycle learning rate schedule. When both decay together, the replay buffer fills with transitions from the committed policy and the network no longer has the effective LR to correct. Each of our three dynamics interventions attacks a different point in this loop: constant LR preserves plasticity, cyclical $\varepsilon$ refills the buffer, and periodic head resets actively de-commit the value function. All three help; head resets help most.

\paragraph{The specific LR--$\varepsilon$ interaction we observed does not appear to be well-characterized in the literature.} Individual phenomena are studied (plasticity loss under LR schedules \citep{Lyle2024}, policy collapse from replay distribution shift \citep{Tarbouriech2025}), but their specific co-occurrence as a failure mode in Rainbow-lite DQN has not been isolated. Investigating whether constant LR is generally preferable to OneCycle for DQN-class algorithms would be a reasonable follow-up.

\paragraph{Why resets beat cyclical $\varepsilon$.} Both interventions inject perturbation on a $500$K schedule. Cyclical $\varepsilon$ injects data diversity; resets inject representation diversity. If the dominant failure mode were lack of exploration, cyclical $\varepsilon$ should have won. If it were network commitment to stale value estimates, resets should have won. Resets winning suggests the latter, consistent with primacy bias being the dominant mechanism rather than pure exploration insufficiency.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The best results rely on early-checkpoint selection: \texttt{model\_best.pt} is the highest single $20$-episode eval, which is a noisy selection criterion. On $100$-episode benchmarks the best-seed numbers hold up, but seed-to-seed variance is substantial (e.g., V8$_\mathrm{fs,reset}$ ranges from $788$ to $872$). A more stable evaluation protocol (e.g., mean of top-$k$ checkpoints) would tighten these numbers.

The environment is a single grid world. Any claim of generality --- about the heuristics H1--H4, about the bottleneck ordering, or about the plasticity fix --- would require replication on additional tasks. We state the findings as a case study.

We did not run an architecture grid over network depth, width, or CNN kernel sizes. With roughly $8$ hours per $2$M-step run and $3$ seeds per configuration, even a modest grid of $10$ architecture variants would cost on the order of $240$ training hours per reward configuration. The saved compute budget was spent on the reward, observation, and dynamics comparisons instead. A targeted architecture search could improve absolute numbers but would not change the qualitative ordering of the bottlenecks.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% =============================================================================

We asked what it takes to make a Rainbow-lite DQN agent consistently match or exceed a scripted heuristic on a multi-objective grid-survival task, and used that target to structure a sequential bottleneck analysis.

Reward shaping turned out to be necessary but not sufficient. We derived four per-step design heuristics (H1--H4) from the environment mechanics, characterized three reward-hacking failure modes (oscillation, hovering, suicide), and systematically tested the resulting configurations. The best reward-only agent reached $706$ survival, below the heuristic's $768$. Scaling individual components, adding compensating penalties, curriculum learning, and extended training all failed to close the gap.

The next bottleneck was the observation. A single 4-frame stack, added without touching the reward function, lifted the best-seed mean survival to $830$ and roughly tripled the agent's foraging rate. The reward had been adequate; temporal context was missing.

The final bottleneck was training dynamics. The frame-stacked policy peaked early and regressed as exploration and learning rate decayed together, a failure mode consistent with recent work on primacy bias and plasticity loss. The simplest fix, a constant learning rate (V7$_\mathrm{fs}$), produces the most consistent result in the study: all three seeds in a tight band around $833$ mean survival, all significantly beating the heuristic. More aggressive interventions (cyclical exploration, Nikishin-style head resets, stronger foraging signals) push the best-seed peak higher, up to $944$ when combined with stronger signals, but each introduces one or two seeds that regress below the heuristic. The cumulative best-seed gain from reward-only E5 ($706$) to V9$_\mathrm{fs,strong\_reset}$ ($944$) is $+238$ ticks, driven by interventions outside the reward function itself. No policy achieves indefinite survival; the longest single episode observed across all experiments is $2{,}477$ ticks.

The specific numbers are tied to this environment, but the methodology --- iteratively identify what is limiting the agent and test the most principled intervention --- transfers. For practitioners building RL agents in multi-objective domains, the ordering reward $\rightarrow$ observation $\rightarrow$ dynamics is a useful default sequence to check, not a generic claim of what always matters.

Future work includes replicating on a second environment to probe the generality of the ordering, sweeping the head-reset frequency around the current $500$K value, and testing whether state-dependent exploration (NoisyNet) can replace both the cyclical schedule and the reset mechanism with a single learned alternative.
